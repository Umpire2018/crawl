{{Politics of the Democratic Republic of the Congo}}
General elections were held in the [[Democratic Republic of the Congo]] on 20 December 2023. Combined elections were held for the [[President of the Democratic Republic of the Congo|President]], 484 of the 500 members of the [[National Assembly of the Democratic Republic of the Congo|National Assembly]], 700 of the 716 elected members of the 26 provincial assemblies, and for the first time under the new constitution, 951 members of a scaled down number of [[Communes of the Democratic Republic of the Congo|commune]] (municipal) councils. On election day CENI, in violation of electoral law, extended voting to 21 December for polling stations that had not opened on 20 December.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/congo-holds-presidential-vote-after-fraught-campaign-2023-12-20/|title=Congo extends chaotic election as opposition calls for rerun|language=en|date=20 December 2023|access-date=20 December 2023|work=[[Reuters]]|first1=Sonia|last1=Rolley|first2=Yassin|last2=Kombi|first3=Fiston Mahamba|last3=Erikas|first4=Mwisi|last4=Kambale|first5=Crispin|last5=Kyala|first6=Arlette|last6=Bashizi|first7=Alessandra|last7=Prentice|first8=Bate|last8=Felix|editor1-first=Alex|editor1-last=Richardson|editor2-first=Toby|editor2-last=Chopra|editor3-first=Sandra|editor3-last=Maler|editor4-first=Daniel|editor4-last=Wallis|location=[[Kinshasa]] and [[Goma]]}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Voting extends to 2nd day in Congo elections amid fraud claims |url=https://www.aa.com.tr/en/africa/voting-extends-to-2nd-day-in-congo-elections-amid-fraud-claims/3088589 |access-date=2023-12-21 |website=Anadolu Ajansi |archive-date=21 December 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20231221090310/https://www.aa.com.tr/en/africa/voting-extends-to-2nd-day-in-congo-elections-amid-fraud-claims/3088589 |url-status=live }}</ref> Some polling stations stayed open on their own accord for up to six extra days.<ref name=":Crisis Group">{{Cite web |date=2024-01-26 |title=DR Congo: A Full Plate of Challenges after a Turbulent Vote {{!}} Crisis Group |url=https://www.crisisgroup.org/africa/great-lakes/democratic-republic-congo/dr-congo-full-plate-challenges-after-turbulent-vote |access-date=2025-02-02 |website=www.crisisgroup.org |language=en}}</ref>

The election saw President [[Félix Tshisekedi]] win in a landslide victory with 73% of the vote, far ahead of his closest rival, former [[Katanga Province]] Governor [[Moïse Katumbi]], who only won 18%, mostly from [[Katanga Province|Katanga region]]. In the parliamentary election, while Tshisekedi's party only won 69 of the seats, his governing coalition, the [[Sacred Union of the Nation]] (USN), won nearly 450, more than 90% of the seats in the [[National Assembly (Democratic Republic of the Congo)|National Assembly]]. As Tshisekedi is dependent on his coalition, he had to compete for other key offices for his party through complex, time consuming negotiations, taking five months to form a government.<ref name=":Crisis Group" /><ref>{{Cite web |title=DR Congo ends impasse to appoint new government |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2024/5/29/dr-congo-ends-impasse-to-appoint-new-government |access-date=2025-02-02 |website=Al Jazeera |language=en}}</ref>

Preparations for the election were marred by significant shortcomings, including the distribution of poorly printed voter cards, many of which had become unreadable by the time of the election as they were issued earlier in the year. Voting was further disrupted by missing voter lists and other essential paperwork, broken or absent machinery, delayed openings of polling stations, and intimidation by security forces or individuals acting on behalf of candidates. These widespread issues contributed to the lowest voter turnout ever recorded, potentially disenfranchising millions of voters.<ref name=":Crisis Group" />

The election, while not excluding any candidate from running and being comparatively peaceful relative to previous elections, had extensive fraud. A domestic observation mission led jointly by the Catholic and various Protestant churches stated that "numerous irregularities affected the integrity of the results of all the polls in some places," such as reports of candidates acquiring voting machines and placing them in their private residences. The opposition called for a rerun of the presidential election but did not take their case to the Constitutional Court, which they see as lacking independence, instead urging street protests that failed to gain momentum. Riots did break out in Katanga over the parliamentary election, as USN members, sometimes fielding no-name candidates, had performed well compared to the presidential election. Katangan elites, excluded from national power in favor of Tshisekedi's allies from his native [[Kasaï region]], are among Tshisekedi's most prominent critics and clashed with Kasaïans, whom many see as "immigrants," over local power in the region. [[International Crisis Group]] (ICG) predicted that Katanga could later oppose Tshisekedi more strongly, as opposition could call for greater power and resources to devolve to provincial authorities or "create momentum behind demands for secession, which [had] been largely rhetorical" prior to the ICG's report.<ref name=":Crisis Group" />

While election irregularities played a part in the opposition's poor performance, other factors included the opposition's failure to unite behind a single candidate, the decision of prominent opposition figures [[Martin Fayulu]] and [[Joseph Kabila]] to have their coalitions sit out the election, their campaign strategies, and the failure to build grassroots party structures in other regions. In contrast, Tshisekedi had the backing of major political figures, which provided him with a broad territorial network and a foothold in various regions of the country.<ref name=":Crisis Group" /><ref name=":conversation" />

CENI later cancelled results in two constituencies and disqualified 82 candidates, mostly members of the USN, from national and local races for fraud. While the move to target mostly USN members may appear to further accountability, analysts,{{who|date=February 2025}} as well as the Catholic Church, stated that it created a precedent for CENI policing its own work and may have underestimated the amount of fraud.<ref name=":Crisis Group" />

Elections were not organized in the [[Territories of the Democratic Republic of the Congo|territories]] of [[Kwamouth Territory|Kwamouth]], [[Masisi Territory|Masisi]], and [[Rutshuru Territory|Rutshuru]] due to ongoing armed conflict.

==Background==
=== 2018 electoral fraud ===
{{Main|2018 Democratic Republic of the Congo general election}}
President Félix Tshisekedi's election in 2018 was extremely controversial, with most independent observers, including the Catholic Church, believing that opposition candidate [[Martin Fayulu]] had actually won in a landslide. They believe that outgoing President [[Joseph Kabila]], realising that a chosen successor candidate couldn't credibly win, struck a deal with Tshisekedi to make him president while Kabila governed jointly with him.<ref name=":ISS" />

According to Jacques Mukena, Senior Governance Researcher at [[Ebuteli Institute]], the election will most likely not be completely free and fair, but believes Tshisekedi and CENI are aware of the fact that they would be under closer scrutiny than in 2018 because more local and international observers would be watching. Additionally, candidates such as [[Delly Sesanga]] have already declared that they would believe the Catholic Church's opinion of who won the elections, not CENI's.<ref name=":ISS" />

According to the [[Crisis Group]], there is a fear of a wider political crisis if losing candidates or their backers do not accept the presidential results. Any crisis, while not inevitable, could worsen the already dire situation in the east.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2023-10-30 |title=Elections in DR Congo: Reducing the Risk of Violence |url=https://www.crisisgroup.org/africa/great-lakes/democratic-republic-congo/312-elections-en-rd-congo-limiter-les-risques-de-violence |access-date=2023-11-21 |website=www.crisisgroup.org |language=en |archive-date=21 November 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20231121180710/https://www.crisisgroup.org/africa/great-lakes/democratic-republic-congo/312-elections-en-rd-congo-limiter-les-risques-de-violence |url-status=live }}</ref>

On 20 November, Fayulu advocated for transparent and impartial elections, he insisted that the Congolese "must no longer accept someone stealing their victory."<ref>{{Cite web |date=2023-11-21 |title=Martin Fayulu à Bagata : " Nous allons avoir une armée de 500 000 personnes bien formées et bien équipée " |url=https://www.radiookapi.net/2023/11/21/actualite/politique/martin-fayulu-bagata-nous-allons-avoir-une-armee-de-500-000-personnes |access-date=2023-11-21 |website=Radio Okapi |language=fr |archive-date=21 November 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20231121180713/https://www.radiookapi.net/2023/11/21/actualite/politique/martin-fayulu-bagata-nous-allons-avoir-une-armee-de-500-000-personnes |url-status=live }}</ref>

===Insecurity===
{{Main|M23 offensive (2022–2023)}}
[[File:M23offensive.png|thumb|325px|Map of the M23 offensive with [[Goma]] on the north shore of [[Lake Kivu]] at the bottom]]
The Democratic Republic of Congo has suffered from almost constant conflict in the east for the past 30 years. More recently, violence surged in the region after a [[M23 offensive (2022–2023)|new rebellion]] by the [[March 23 Movement|M23 group]], supported by [[Rwanda]],  caused much of the [[North Kivu]] province to be occupied by rebels. This upsurge in violence comes as [[MONUSCO]] is expected to begin its "accelerated" withdrawal, as requested by Tshisekedi, after an almost 25-year presence in the country.<ref name=":1">{{Cite web |date=2023-11-19 |title=DRC: campaigning for presidential elections officially launched |url=https://www.africanews.com/2023/11/19/drc-campaigning-for-presidential-elections-officially-launched/ |access-date=2023-11-21 |website=Africanews |language=en |archive-date=21 November 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20231121180709/https://www.africanews.com/2023/11/19/drc-campaigning-for-presidential-elections-officially-launched/ |url-status=live }}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |date=2023-11-16 |title=DR Congo faces logistical, security challenges before pivotal December poll |url=https://www.france24.com/en/africa/20231116-dr-congo-faces-logistical-security-challenges-before-pivotal-december-poll |access-date=2023-11-21 |website=France 24 |language=en |archive-date=21 November 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20231121180715/https://www.france24.com/en/africa/20231116-dr-congo-faces-logistical-security-challenges-before-pivotal-december-poll |url-status=live }}</ref>

Due to this, two [[Territories of the Democratic Republic of the Congo|territories]] of the province will not be able to vote normally, but if [[Goma]] were to fall as it did in [[M23 rebellion (2012–2013)|2012]], the whole process would be compromised.<ref name=":1" />

With the possibility of over a million voters being disenfranchised from instability, the [[Southern African Development Community]] (SADC) has proposed sending a regional intervention force into eastern DRC to try to stabilise the area. The SADC Mission in the DRC was first proposed in May and was meant to go in by September but has been postponed, with its most recent summit concerning finance.<ref name=":ISS" />

According to the [[Institute for Security Studies]], it's hard to imagine the mission could go in and suppress all of eastern DRC's many armed rebel groups in time to enfranchise those voters.<ref name=":ISS" />

==Electoral system==

===Presidential election===
The president is elected by [[plurality voting]] in one round.<ref>{{cite web |title=Constitution de la Republique Democratique du Congo |trans-title=The Constitution of the Democratic Republic of the Congo |date=20 January 2011 |at=Article 71 |url=https://leganet.cd/Legislation/Droit%20Public/Constitution.2011.pdf#page=23 |website=LEGANET.CD |language=fr |access-date=20 July 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190221223039/https://leganet.cd/Legislation/Droit%20Public/Constitution.2011.pdf |archive-date=21 February 2019 |url-status=live}}</ref>  For the first time, some Congolese living abroad were able to vote in the presidential election.  These were those living in Belgium, Canada, France, South Africa, and the United States.

At the start of every five year election cycle voter registration takes place.  The results for each province, including Kinshasa, are first used to proportionally distribute the 500 National Assembly seats and the 780 total seats of the provincial assemblies to the provinces. This determines the size of the provincial delegation in the National Assembly and the size of each provincial assembly.  The second phase proportionally allocates provincial seats to each assembly district—in the case of the provincial assemblies, up to 10% of seats are reserved for the [[co-option]] of traditional leaders and are not allocated to an assembly district.

For the 2023 election, voter registration could not be carried out in some areas of the territories of [[Kwamouth Territory|Kwamouth]], [[Masisi Territory|Masisi]], and [[Rutshuru Territory|Rutshuru]] due to armed conflict.  To deal with this, it was decided to postpone the elections in these territories, to reserve the same number of seats for these districts as they had in 2018, and to proportionally distribute the remaining seats to the other districts. The result was that only 484 National Assembly seats and 700 provincial assembly seats were to be contested.

[[Lumumbaville]] elected its first [[National Assembly of the Democratic Republic of the Congo|National Assembly]] deputy and its first deputy to the Provincial Assembly of Sankuru. This was the only new [[legislative district]] of the 2024-2028 legislature.<ref>{{cite web |title=Liste des meilleurs élus aux législatives nationales de décembre 2023 |url=https://www.ceni.cd/sites/default/files/2024-01/CENI%20RDC%20-%20Liste%20des%20Meilleurs%20Elus%20à%20la%20Deputation%20Nationale%20-%20Resultats%20Provisoires%20Election%20Leg.pdf#page=7 |trans-title= List of those elected with absolute majorities in the National Assembly elections of December 2023 |publisher=[[Independent National Electoral Commission (Democratic Republic of the Congo)|CENI]] |date= 19 January 2024 |page=7 |access-date=19 March 2024 |language=fr}}; {{cite news |title=Voici les députés provinciaux élus: Nord-Kivu, Nord-Ubangi, Sankuru, Sud-Kivu, Sud-Ubangi, Tanganyka, Tshopo et Tshuapa |url=https://www.forumdesas.net/2024/01/voici-les-deputes-provinciaux-elus-nord-kivu-nord-ubangi-sankuru-sud-kivu-sud-ubangi-tanganyka-tshopo-et-tshuapa/ |access-date=19 March 2024 |work=Forum des As |date=24 January 2024 |language=fr}}</ref>

In the case of a commune council election, the commune is the single multi-member electoral district with the number of members determined by the number of registered voters in the commune according to a fixed table.

====Getting on the ballot====
Each candidate for these elections are part of a three-person [[ticket (elections)|ticket]] which includes candidates for first and second [[substitute (elections)|substitute]].  It is not unusual for a candidate to run for both a national and provincial assembly seat in which case they can keep but one and a substitute takes the other.

A new rule requires participating political parties and [[Electoral alliance|alliances]] to contest at least 60% of the seats up for renewal in an election.  So for example, each party/alliance had to register at least 290 candidates to participate in the National Assembly election.<ref>{{cite news |title=RDC: plus de 20.000 candidatures pour 484 sièges à l'Assemblée |url=https://afrique.le360.ma/politique/rdc-plus-de-20000-candidatures-pour-484-sieges-a-lassemblee_K2RAYXJOGRDLFL46EGNY4O2474/ |access-date=6 January 2024 |work=Le360 Afrique |agency=with [[Agence France-Presse|AFP]] |date=11 August 2023 |language=fr |archive-date=25 September 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230925040335/https://afrique.le360.ma/politique/rdc-plus-de-20000-candidatures-pour-484-sieges-a-lassemblee_K2RAYXJOGRDLFL46EGNY4O2474/ |url-status=live }}</ref>

====Election method====
The method by which members are elected are different in districts having more than one seat, the most common case, from those that end up with only one seat.<ref name=kadima>{{cite news |title=Elections 2023 : 688 candidats élus provisoirement députés provinciaux (CENI) |url=https://www.radiookapi.net/2024/01/22/actualite/politique/elections-2023-688-candidats-elus-provisoirement-deputes-provinciaux |access-date=23 January 2024 |work=[[Radio Okapi]] |date=22 January 2024 |language=fr}}</ref>

In multiple-member districts, members are elected by [[open list]] [[proportional representation]], with seats assigned using the [[largest remainder method]].  Candidates who win more than half the vote in their district are automatically assigned a seat.<ref>{{cite news |title=RDC: Carole Agito, Matata Ponyo et Sakombi Molendo parmi les députés les mieux élus |url=https://actualite.cd/2024/01/14/rdc-carole-agito-matata-ponyo-et-sakombi-molendo-parmi-les-deputes-les-mieux-elus |access-date=20 January 2024 |work=Actualite.cd |date=14 January 2024 |language=fr}}</ref>  Otherwise, a party or independent candidate must meet an [[election threshold]] to qualify for seat assignment.<ref name=kadima />  The election thresholds for the National Assembly, a provincial assembly, or a commune council are 1% of the vote nationally, 3% provincially, and 10% in the commune, respectively.<ref>{{cite web |title=Pour obtenir un siège dans les assemblées, le nombre de voix ne suffit pas |url=https://www.studiohirondellerdc.org/pour-obtenir-un-siege-dans-les-assemblees-le-nombre-de-voix-ne-suffit-pas |website=Studio Hirondelle-RDC |access-date=20 January 2024 |language=fr |date=9 January 2024}}</ref>

In single member districts, members are elected using [[first-past-the-post voting]].<ref name=kadima />

==Opinion polls==
Opinion polling is rare in the Democratic Republic of the Congo due to poor roads and lack of electricity. Nevertheless, a survey conducted by [[GeoPoll Socio-Political Barometer]] in the second quarter of 2023 found that voters expressed significant discontent with the governance under Tshisekedi, assigning him a satisfaction rating of 49.7%. Despite this, the survey also suggested that Tshisekedi would secure a second term thanks in part to perceived improvements, such as his free education initiative, and partly due to a divided opposition.<ref name=":ISS">{{Cite web |date=2023-11-03 |title=Is DRC heading for another chaotic election? |url=https://issafrica.org/iss-today/is-drc-heading-for-another-chaotic-election |access-date=2023-11-21 |website=ISS Africa |language=en |archive-date=21 November 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20231121180715/https://issafrica.org/iss-today/is-drc-heading-for-another-chaotic-election |url-status=live }}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |last=Kibuacha |first=Frankline |date=2023-08-23 |title=The GeoPoll Socio-Political Barometer Survey DRC - Q2 2023 Report |url=https://www.geopoll.com/blog/drc-socio-political-barometer-q2-2023/ |access-date=2023-11-21 |website=GeoPoll |language=en-US |archive-date=21 November 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20231121180715/https://www.geopoll.com/blog/drc-socio-political-barometer-q2-2023/ |url-status=live }}</ref>

An earlier 2022 poll by the same group found unemployment and insecurity to be the most cited dysfunctional areas, closely followed by the state of the country's roads and rising prices. On the other hand, free education was found to be the most favorable policy.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Angus-Hammond |first=Matt |date=2022-12-23 |title=Democratic Republic of the Congo - The GeoPoll Socio-Political Barometer Survey - Report Preview |url=https://www.geopoll.com/blog/socio-political-barometer-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-report-preview/ |access-date=2023-11-21 |website=GeoPoll |language=en-US |archive-date=21 November 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20231121180711/https://www.geopoll.com/blog/socio-political-barometer-democratic-republic-of-the-congo-report-preview/ |url-status=live }}</ref>

== Conduct ==

=== Pre-election violence ===
While the lead-up to the election was generally calm, several violent incidents were reported. On 14 July 2023, [[Chérubin Okende Senga]], spokesperson for ENSEMBLE and former transport minister, was fatally shot in [[Kinshasa]]. The murder, described by Katumbi as a "political assassination," led to legal action by Senga's relatives in [[Brussels]], accusing the head of the DRC's military intelligence of involvement.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2023-07-14 |title=La Prospérité : " Chérubin Okende : les Evêques catholiques exigent la tête de Caïn ! " |url=https://www.radiookapi.net/2023/07/14/actualite/revue-de-presse/la-prosperite-cherubin-okende-les-eveques-catholiques-exigent |access-date=2024-01-18 |website=Radio Okapi |language=fr}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Relatives of slain Congolese politician file suit in Belgium against Intelligence chief |url=https://www.brusselstimes.com/787972/relatives-of-slain-congolese-politician-file-suit-in-belgium-against-intelligence-chief |access-date=2024-01-18 |website=The Brussels Times |language=en}}</ref> Later in the year, a Katumbi rally in [[Moanda, Democratic Republic of the Congo|Moanda]] was broken up by live rounds, injuring several people. The provincial government blamed Katumbi's guards, stating that they fired warning shots after the crowd grew rowdy. Katumbi blamed the police.<ref>{{Cite web |last1=Christensen |first1=Sofia |last2=Kasongo |first2=Ange |title=Congo opposition candidate suspends presidential campaign after violence |url=https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/congo-opposition-candidate-suspends-presidential-campaign-after-violence-2023-12-13/ |website=[[Reuters]]}}</ref>

Violent clashes between supporters of different parties were also observed across various provinces, with candidates facing death threats on the campaign trail. On November 4, suspected UDPS activists attacked the convoy of Martin Fayulu in [[Tshikapa]], [[Kasaï Province|Kasai province]]. On November 7, UDPS supporters clashed with ENSEMBLE supporters at a rally in [[Kasumbalesa, Democratic Republic of the Congo|Kasumbalesa]], followed by ransacking of the local UDPS headquarters by Ensemble supporters. 

On November 28, during Katumbi's march in [[Kindu]], UDPS supporters threw stones at Dido Kakisingi, leader of ENSEMBLE's Maniema youth league, to then be run over and killed by a truck belonging to the campaign team of [[Maniema]] Governor [[Afani Idrissa Mangala]].<ref name="hrw kindu">{{Cite web | website=HRW|language=en-GB| url=https://www.hrw.org/news/2023/12/16/dr-congo-electoral-violence-threatens-vote|title=DR Congo: Electoral Violence Threatens Vote|date=2023-12-16|access-date=2024-12-20}}</ref> After his killing, more UDPS supporters were seen throwing stones, and gunshots were recorded, presumably from the Congolese National Police.<ref name="bellingcat kindu">{{Cite web |last1=Stoecklin |first1=Sarah|last2=Giannardi|first2=Riccardo |date=2024-12-10 |title=Retracing the Congolese March Where Dido Kakisingi was Killed |url=https://www.bellingcat.com/news/2024/12/10/retracing-the-congolese-march-where-dido-kakisingi-was-killed/ |website=Bellingcat |language=en-GB|access-date=2024-12-20}}</ref> Two people were sentenced to five years in jail in relation to the killing of Kakisingi. 

At least 19 deaths, including two candidates, have been attributed to election-related violence.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2023-12-23 |title=Carter Center preliminary statement on the Democratic Republic of the Congo general elections - Democratic Republic of the Congo {{!}} ReliefWeb |url=https://reliefweb.int/report/democratic-republic-congo/carter-center-preliminary-statement-democratic-republic-congo-general-elections |access-date=2024-01-18 |website=reliefweb.int |language=en}}</ref>

=== Election preparations ===
CENI was reportedly woefully underprepared for the election. Due to the state of the country's roads compared to its size and the lack of funding, CENI was forced to resort to doing almost everything via plane. As transporting by air is costly, CENI had to get Egypt to send two [[Lockheed C-130 Hercules|C-130 Hercules]] planes to help deliver ballot papers at the last moment. Additionally, CENI begged the UN to use its aircraft.<ref name=":4">{{Cite web |last=Niarchos |first=Nicolas |title='A Simulacrum of Elections' {{!}} Nicolas Niarchos |url=https://www.nybooks.com/online/2024/01/16/a-simulacrum-of-elections/ |access-date=2024-01-18 |website=The New York Review of Books |date=16 January 2024 |language=en}}</ref>

=== Election day ===
On election day voting offices were scheduled to open at 6 AM, but delays were observed nationwide, resulting in the formation of exceptionally long lines. Various logistical issues further compounded the situation, including the late arrival of materials, malfunctioning voting machines, failed batteries intended to sustain their operation, and instances of lost ballot papers. This prolonged waiting period reportedly led to frustration among poorly informed and/or impatient individuals, resulting in attacks on poll workers and polling stations. Additionally, 11,000 voting stations didn't even vote at all or were not counted.<ref name=":4" />

According to Schadrack Mukad, an adjunct executive national secretary of the [[Civil Society Organization for Peace in Congo]], which deployed 75,000 observers during the vote, "there were cases of machines that were seized by certain candidates and others by certain agents of CENI outside voting places.” He expressed concern about the involvement of certain politico-administrative authorities and electoral candidates, who he says diverted CENI agents away from polling stations for a significant duration. Mukad attributed these violations to members affiliated with Tshisekedi's coalition.<ref name=":4" />

Controversially, the election necessitated an extension into a second day, a move which was declared illegal by local observers and civil society, and parts of the country were still casting ballots five days after election day.<ref name=":5">{{Cite web |title=DR Congo's President Tshisekedi sworn in for second term amid disputes |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2024/1/20/dr-congos-president-tshisekedi-sworn-in-for-second-term-amid-disputes |access-date=2024-01-20 |website=Al Jazeera |language=en}}</ref>

CENI recognised cases of fraud, vandalism and intimidation, as well as the use of illegal voting machines.<ref name=":5" />

=== Analysis ===
According to [[Nicolas Niarchos]], in his piece for [[The New York Review of Books]], CENI's polling station data, "although impressive in detail", showed "strange" results. In the Fayulu stronghold of Kinshasa, for example, only 1,756,303 votes were counted–just ten percent of the capital's population.<ref name=":4" />

[[Tafi Mhaka]], in an opinion piece for [[Al Jazeera Media Network|Aljazeera]], described the elections as "[[Sham election|shambolic]]," calling for the [[Southern African Development Community]] to uphold electoral standards in every single member country.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Mhaka |first=Tafi |title=DR Congo's shambolic election should be a wake-up call for the SADC |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2024/1/18/dr-congos-shambolic-election-should-be-a-wake-up-call-for-the-sadc |access-date=2024-01-20 |website=Al Jazeera |language=en}}</ref>

Alternatively, [[Albert Kasanda]], in his piece for [[The Conversation (website)|The Conversation]], partially attributes the opposition's failure to unite behind a single candidate and their campaign strategies, compared to Tshisekedi, who had the backing of major political figures which provided him a broad territorial network and a foothold in various regions of the country.<ref name=":conversation">{{Cite web |last=Kasanda |first=Albert |date=2024-01-17 |title=DR Congo's election – the key to Félix Tshisekedi's victory |url=http://theconversation.com/dr-congos-election-the-key-to-felix-tshisekedis-victory-221143 |access-date=2024-01-20 |website=The Conversation |language=en-US}}</ref>

==Aftermath==
On 6 January 2024, Katumbi released a statement disputing the results of the election on the grounds of "massive fraud and treachery" and calling for the resignation of Denis Kadima, the head of the [[Independent National Electoral Commission (Democratic Republic of the Congo)|Independent National Electoral Commission]]. Two days later, his party stated that he had been placed under house arrest, with a spokesman reporting the presence of "heavily armed soldiers traveling in armoured vehicles surrounding his house".<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.newtimes.co.rw/article/13626/news/africa/dr-congo-opposition-politician-moise-katumbi-blocked-from-leaving-home|title=DR Congo opposition politician Moïse Katumbi blocked from leaving home|newspaper=The New Times|location=Rwanda|date=9 January 2024|access-date=10 January 2024|archive-date=10 January 2024|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20240110005622/https://www.newtimes.co.rw/article/13626/news/africa/dr-congo-opposition-politician-moise-katumbi-blocked-from-leaving-home|url-status=live}}</ref> The security forces were subsequently ordered to withdraw by the provincial governor [[Jacques Kyabula Katwe]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/world-africa-67920501|title=Moïse Katumbi in DR Congo: Troops surrounded poll loser's home|publisher=BBC News|date=9 January 2024|access-date=10 January 2024|archive-date=10 January 2024|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20240110010103/https://www.bbc.com/news/world-africa-67920501|url-status=live}}</ref>
