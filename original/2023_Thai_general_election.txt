{{Politics of Thailand}}

General elections were held in [[Thailand]] on 14 May 2023 to elect 500 members of the [[House of Representatives (Thailand)|House of Representatives]].<ref>{{Cite web |date=20 March 2023 |title=พระราชกฤษฎีกายุบสภาผู้แทนราษฎร พ.ศ. 2566" |url=https://ratchakitcha.soc.go.th/documents/140A022N0000000000100.pdf |access-date=21 March 2023 |archive-date=20 March 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230320164814/https://ratchakitcha.soc.go.th/documents/140A022N0000000000100.pdf |url-status=live }}</ref> The [[Move Forward Party]], led by [[Pita Limjaroenrat]], surprised analysts by winning the most seats, followed by fellow opposition party [[Pheu Thai Party|Pheu Thai]] who had won the most seats in the [[2011 Thai general election|2011]] and [[2019 Thai general election|2019]] elections. Turnout was a record 75.22%.<ref name=":1">{{Cite news |title=Election Commission says MFP won Sunday's election |language=en |work=Bangkok Post |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2570334/election-commission-says-mfp-won-sundays-election |access-date=15 May 2023}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |date=15 May 2023 |title=Thailand's Election Commission declares victory for Move Forward Party |url=https://www.thaipbsworld.com/thailands-election-commission-declares-victory-for-move-forward-party/ |access-date=16 May 2023 |work=Thai PBS World}}</ref>

The elections were held using a [[parallel voting]] system as in the [[1997 constitution of Thailand|1997]] and [[2007 constitution of Thailand|2007 constitutions]], in contrast to the [[Mixed-member proportional representation|mixed-member apportionment system]] used in 2019. Like in 2019, the elected prime minister did not need to be a member of the House and would be chosen by the full [[National Assembly (Thailand)|National Assembly]], including the 250 military-appointed members of the [[Senate of Thailand|Senate]], for a total 376 seat majority. Due to the structure of the National Assembly, experts had warned of a possible [[Gridlock (politics)|gridlock]] scenario in which the current opposition parties secure more than half of the House votes but are obstructed in the Senate. Additionally, the Election Commission had received criticism over untrustworthiness, unprofessionalism, and bias in its conducting of the voting process.

Sixty-seven parties vied for seats. The ruling conservative [[coalition government]] was composed of the major [[Palang Pracharath Party|Palang Pracharath]], [[Bhumjai Thai Party|Bhumjai Thai]], and [[Democrat Party (Thailand)|Democrat]] parties, and smaller parties including the new [[United Thai Nation Party]], who nominated former junta leader and incumbent prime minister [[Prayut Chan-o-cha]] after his split with Palang Pracharath. The pro-democratic opposition was led by the Pheu Thai and Move Forward parties, the latter being the effective successor of the dissolved [[Future Forward Party]], which had performed unexpectedly well in the [[2019 Thai general election|2019 election]]. Political campaigns focused on the [[Thai economy]], especially its recovery from the [[COVID-19 pandemic in Thailand|COVID-19 pandemic]]. Some parties, most notably Move Forward, also highlighted [[progressivism|progressive]] issues,<ref>{{cite news |title=Thailand's victorious progressive Move Forward Party, 7 allies agree on coalition platform |url=https://apnews.com/article/thailand-election-move-forward-coalition-parties-0380ff3fe653e1e43fcc7e0f7c3c81a1 |access-date=26 May 2023 |work=AP NEWS|first=Jintamas|last=Saksornchai |date=22 May 2023 |language=en}}</ref> and challenged long-held social norms in Thailand.

Pita's Move Forward initially formed a coalition with the other pro-democracy, anti-military parties, which had a majority in the lower house but was unable to form a government after being functionally blocked by allies of the monarchy and military in the Senate. Pheu Thai then assumed the lead, dissolving its alliance with Move Forward and allying instead with conservative, pro-military parties. It then nominated real estate tycoon [[Srettha Thavisin]] as prime minister. He was elected by Parliament on 22 August.<ref>{{cite news|first1=Sui-Lee|last1=Wee|first2=Muktita|last2=Suhartono|title=Thai Parliament Picks Real Estate Mogul as Next Prime Minister|date=21 August 2023|newspaper=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2023/08/21/world/asia/thailand-thaksin-prime-minister.html|access-date=23 August 2023}}</ref> 

On August 7, 2024, Thailand's [[Constitutional Court of Thailand|Constitutional Court]] banned the victors of the election, the Move Forward Party and all of its leaders from politics for its proposal to reform the [[lèse-majesté in Thailand|lèse-majesté law]], arguing it posed a threat to the constitutional order.<ref name="Economist1">{{Cite web |date=7 August 2024 |title=Thailand's top court tramples over the country's democracy |url=https://www.economist.com/asia/2024/08/07/thailands-top-court-tramples-over-the-countrys-democracy |access-date=17 August 2024 |archive-date=16 August 2024 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20240816203416/https://www.economist.com/asia/2024/08/07/thailands-top-court-tramples-over-the-countrys-democracy |url-status=live}}</ref>

==Background==
Following a [[2013–2014 Thai political crisis|political crisis]] in [[Thailand]], the military staged [[2014 Thai coup d'état|a coup d'état in 2014]], ousting [[Yingluck cabinet|the civilian caretaker government]] of [[Yingluck Shinawatra]]. The military junta, known as the [[National Council for Peace and Order|National Council for Peace and Order (NCPO)]], rose to power under the leadership of former army chief [[Prayut Chan-o-cha]] as [[Prime Minister of Thailand|Prime Minister]]. In 2016, the NCPO completed the drafting of [[2017 constitution of Thailand|a new constitution]] and held [[2016 Thai constitutional referendum|a referendum]] to approve it. They banned criticism of the draft constitution and prohibited monitoring of the referendum. Activists against the document were arrested, detained, and prosecuted in military courts,<ref>{{cite web |date=21 June 2016 |title=Thailand: Junta Bans Referendum Monitoring |url=https://www.hrw.org/news/2016/06/21/thailand-junta-bans-referendum-monitoring |accessdate=5 July 2016 |website=Human Rights Watch |location=New York |archive-date=21 August 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20220821034003/https://www.hrw.org/news/2016/06/21/thailand-junta-bans-referendum-monitoring |url-status=live }}</ref> whilst voters who expressed their intention to vote against the draft were also arrested and prosecuted by the military regime.<ref>{{cite web |date=17 July 2016 |title=ทนายดังมอบตัวสู้คดีทำผิด พ.ร.บ. ออกเสียงประชามติ |url=http://www.matichon.co.th/news/214333 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160719161817/http://www.matichon.co.th/news/214333 |archive-date=19 July 2016 |accessdate=17 July 2016 |website=Matichon Online |publisher={{Interlanguage link multi|มติชน|th}} |location=Bangkok |language=th}}</ref>

In 2019, after numerous delays, the junta finally held [[2019 Thai general election|a general election]] on 24 March. The elections were seen as a skewed race in which Prayut had an unfair advantage, with the [[Senate of Thailand|Senate]] wholly appointed by the junta and the constituencies redrawn last-minute.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Thai Senate to be appointed at junta's behest |url=https://asia.nikkei.com/Politics/Thai-Senate-to-be-appointed-at-junta-s-behest |access-date=18 March 2022 |website=Nikkei Asia |language=en-GB |archive-date=18 March 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20220318053640/https://asia.nikkei.com/Politics/Thai-Senate-to-be-appointed-at-junta-s-behest |url-status=live }}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |last1=Rojanaphruk |first1=Pravit |last2=Writer |first2=Senior Staff |date=30 November 2018 |title=Parties Fume Over New 'Gerrymandered' Electoral Map |url=https://www.khaosodenglish.com/politics/2018/11/30/parties-fume-over-gerrymandered-electoral-map/ |access-date=17 March 2022 |website=Khaosod English |language=en-US |archive-date=5 June 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20220605103243/https://www.khaosodenglish.com/politics/2018/11/30/parties-fume-over-gerrymandered-electoral-map/ |url-status=live }}</ref> After the election, the pro-junta [[Palang Pracharath Party|Palang Pracharath party]] formed a [[coalition government]], with Prayut selected by the parliament for another term as prime minister even though his party did not win the most seats.<ref name="WSJ">[https://www.wsj.com/articles/young-harvard-educated-politician-shakes-up-thailands-old-guard-235d1f18?mod=world_lead_pos4 Young, Harvard-Educated Politician Shakes Up Thailand's Old Guard], ''The Wall Street Journal''</ref>

Prayut began his second term as prime minister on 9 June 2019.<ref>{{Cite news |title=Prayut counts on his allies |work=Bangkok Post |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2266927/prayut-counts-on-his-allies |access-date=18 March 2022}}</ref> According to the current constitution, a prime minister can only serve for a maximum of eight years. However, the end of Prayut's term as the prime minister of Thailand is disputed since there are many interpretations about the beginning of his term.<ref name=":0">{{Cite news |title=PM defies efforts to oust him from office |work=Bangkok Post |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2305990/pm-defies-efforts-to-oust-him-from-office |access-date=8 May 2022}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |last1=Rojanaphruk |first1=Pravit |last2=Writer |first2=Senior Staff |date=30 December 2021 |title=Prawit Says He's Willing to Serve With Prayut Till '2027' |url=https://www.khaosodenglish.com/politics/2021/12/30/prawit-says-hes-willing-to-serve-with-prayut-till-2027/ |access-date=18 March 2022 |website=Khaosod English |language=en-US |archive-date=8 April 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20220408182358/https://www.khaosodenglish.com/politics/2021/12/30/prawit-says-hes-willing-to-serve-with-prayut-till-2027/ |url-status=live }}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |date=29 December 2021 |title=ฝ่าย ก.ม. ชี้วาระ 8 ปี 'ประยุทธ์' ลากยาวได้ถึงปี 2570 |url=https://www.voicetv.co.th/read/x1s7v7AhM |access-date=18 March 2022 |website=VoiceTV |language=th |archive-date=29 December 2021 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20211229202436/https://voicetv.co.th/read/x1s7v7AhM |url-status=live }}</ref> On 30 September 2022, the [[Constitutional Court of Thailand|Constitutional Court]] finally ruled that Prayut's term began in 2017 along with the new constitution, meaning that he may serve as the prime minister until 2025, if he is selected by the parliament again.

The [[2020–2021 Thai protests|2020–2021 nationwide protests]] started as demonstrations against the premiership of Prayuth but later expanded to include numerous long-overlooked issues in Thai society, and unprecedented demands to reform the monarchy. The Thai royal family is protected by [[Lèse-majesté in Thailand|a lèse-majesté law]]. Even though the protests largely died down due to crackdown, repression, and COVID-19 restrictions, it was anticipated that activism could resurge in this election and afterwards.<ref>{{cite news |title=Thailand: What happened to mass anti-government protests? {{!}} DW {{!}} 19 May 2022 |url=https://www.dw.com/en/thailand-what-happened-to-mass-anti-government-protests/a-61861179 |access-date=8 June 2022 |work=DW |archive-date=5 June 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20220605131101/https://www.dw.com/en/thailand-what-happened-to-mass-anti-government-protests/a-61861179 |url-status=live }}</ref> 

The end of 2022 saw the split in the ruling Palang Pracharath Party between Prayut and his close associate [[Prawit Wongsuwon]], after the latter showed accommodation towards the main opposition [[Pheu Thai Party]]. Prayut was expected to join the new [[United Thai Nation Party]] along with his loyalists in the Palang Pracharath Party.<ref>{{cite news |title=Thai Political Parties: No Eternal Allies, No Perpetual Enemies |url=https://thediplomat.com/2022/12/thai-political-parties-no-eternal-allies-no-perpetual-enemies/ |access-date=21 December 2022 |work=The Diplomat |archive-date=21 December 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20221221054816/https://thediplomat.com/2022/12/thai-political-parties-no-eternal-allies-no-perpetual-enemies/ |url-status=live }}</ref> Forty politicians, including 34 incumbent MPs from both coalition and opposition camps, also resigned from their parties to join [[Bhumjai Thai Party]] to increase their winning chances in this election.<ref>{{cite news |title=Former MPs jump ship, join Bhumjai Thai |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2462152/former-mps-jump-ship-join-bhumjai-thai |access-date=21 December 2022 |work=Bangkok Post |language=en}}</ref> On 23 December 2022, Prayut announced his intention to apply for membership of the United Thai Nation Party, as well as becoming the party's sole prime ministerial candidate.<ref>{{Cite web |last=isarin |date=23 December 2022 |title=ด่วน ประยุทธ์ ประกาศสมัครสมาชิกพรรครวมไทยสร้างชาติ |url=https://www.prachachat.net/prachachat-top-story/news-1157022 |access-date=23 December 2022 |website=ประชาชาติธุรกิจ |language=th |archive-date=23 December 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20221223070503/https://www.prachachat.net/prachachat-top-story/news-1157022 |url-status=live }}</ref>

Rumor of a possible coup was circulated long before the election. In September 2022, a cabinet minister, [[Chaiwut Thanakamanusorn]], warned that protesting may result in the cancellation of the election.<ref>{{Cite web |date=24 September 2022 |title="ถ้าเคลื่อนไหวมาก ระวังจะไม่ได้เลือกตั้ง" ชัยวุฒิเตือนม็อบเคลื่อนไหว วันศาล รธน. อ่านคำวินิจฉัยปม 8 ปี นายกฯ |url=https://thestandard.co/chaiwut-thanakamanusorn-240965/ |access-date=27 January 2023 |website=THE STANDARD |language=th |archive-date=27 January 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230127102229/https://thestandard.co/chaiwut-thanakamanusorn-240965/ |url-status=live }}</ref>

==Electoral system==
[[File:14-05-23 --- a Bangkok polling station for 2023 Thailand General Election - IMG 03.jpg|thumb|right|A polling station in [[Bangkok]] on 14 May 2023, note the two separate boxes for each of the ballots]]
Unlike the preceding [[2019 Thai general election|2019 election]], which used a form of [[mixed-member proportional representation]] with 350 constituency seats and the remaining 150 being [[levelling seat]]s, the electoral system was changed in a 2021 amendment of the constitution which restored the pre-2017 [[parallel voting]] system and removed the [[proportional representation]] mechanism.

Of the 500 members of the [[House of Representatives (Thailand)|House of Representatives]] to be elected, 400 seats were elected from single-member constituencies by [[first-past-the-post voting]] (an increase of 50), and 100 party-list seats—filled separately and no longer serving as leveling seats—are voted on in a separate ballot (unlike in the 2019 election, where only one vote was cast by each voter to determine both constituency and levelling seats).<ref>{{cite news |title=Thai Parliament passes election changes favouring ruling party |url=https://www.businesstimes.com.sg/government-economy/thai-parliament-passes-election-changes-favouring-ruling-party |access-date=26 June 2020 |work=The Business Times |date=10 September 2021 |language=en |archive-date=13 September 2021 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210913082555/https://www.businesstimes.com.sg/government-economy/thai-parliament-passes-election-changes-favouring-ruling-party |url-status=live }}</ref> The change was criticised by smaller parties as the system benefits larger parties—especially the ruling Palang Pracharath Party and the main opposition party Pheu Thai, who both supported the amendment—at the expense of smaller ones, including the progressive [[Move Forward Party]], whose predecessor [[Future Forward Party|Future Forward]] performed successfully in 2019 thanks to the proportional representation system.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Yuda |first1=Masayuki |title=Thai ruling party irks progressives with electoral tweaks |url=https://asia.nikkei.com/Politics/Turbulent-Thailand/Thai-ruling-party-irks-progressives-with-electoral-tweaks |access-date=23 November 2022 |work=Nikkei Asia |date=16 September 2021 |archive-date=23 November 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20221123102936/https://asia.nikkei.com/Politics/Turbulent-Thailand/Thai-ruling-party-irks-progressives-with-electoral-tweaks |url-status=live }}</ref>

=== Prime ministerial candidates ===
The process of selecting a prime minister is still the same as the preceding 2019 election under the [[Constitution of Thailand|2017 Thai constitution]]. Prime ministers may only be chosen from a pre-declared list of candidates. Each party may submit up to three names and must have at least 25 members in the House of Representatives to receive eligibility. Candidates do not have to be a [[member of parliament]] (MP).

Parliament's vote for prime minister will take place in a joint session with the 250-seat [[Senate of Thailand|Senate]] appointed by the junta, according to the constitution's provisional terms. In 2019, all the senators unanimously voted for then-junta leader Prayut.<ref>{{Cite web |date=5 June 2019 |title=Thailand's military-backed PM voted in after junta creates loose coalition |url=http://www.theguardian.com/world/2019/jun/05/thailand-military-backed-pm-prayuth-chan-ocha-voted-in-after-junta-creates-loose-coalition |access-date=27 January 2023 |website=the Guardian |language=en |archive-date=7 April 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230407035757/https://www.theguardian.com/world/2019/jun/05/thailand-military-backed-pm-prayuth-chan-ocha-voted-in-after-junta-creates-loose-coalition |url-status=live }}</ref> As the term of the NCPO-appointed Senate lasts until 2024, it is expected to exert influence into this election as well.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Thongnoi |first1=Jitsiree |last2=Jaipragas |first2=Bhavan |title=Thai election a battle royale for junta's Prayuth and the Shinawatras |url=https://www.scmp.com/week-asia/politics/article/3002722/explained-thailands-election-battle-royale-juntas-prayuth-chan |access-date=26 June 2020 |work=South China Morning Post |date=22 March 2019 |language=en |archive-date=28 June 2020 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200628112658/https://www.scmp.com/week-asia/politics/article/3002722/explained-thailands-election-battle-royale-juntas-prayuth-chan |url-status=live }}</ref>

Due to this rule, the anti-junta camp had to secure at least 376 votes in order to select their candidates,<ref name="WSJ"/> while a simple majority in the House could still result in a candidate from the pro-junta coalition winning the selection. If no candidates secured at least 376 votes in the Parliament, it could lead to a deadlock<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.wsj.com/articles/thailand-pro-democracy-candidates-push-to-break-military-grip-in-election-f44a367f?mod=article_inline|title=Thai Voters Back Pro-Democracy Parties in Blow to Military|work=The Wall Street Journal|first=Feliz|last=Solomon|date=14 May 2023|access-date=3 June 2023}}</ref> and pave the way for a person not in the parties' list of candidates becoming prime minister as written in the Constitution. Such a scenario led some to call for [[strategic voting]] for larger anti-junta parties most likely to win in each constituency.

===2022 reapportionment and redistricting===
Due to the increase of the number of members of the House of Representatives elected from single-member constituencies from 350 to 400, a reapportionment of seats and redrawing of electoral boundaries was required. On 1 February 2022, the [[Election Commission of Thailand|Election Commission]] announced its calculations that, from the total registered population of 66,171,439 as of 31 December 2021, there must be 1 member per 165,429 people. This distribution is subject to the approval of the organic law bills currently being considered by the National Assembly.<ref>{{Cite web |title=เปิดจำนวน ส.ส. 400 เขตเลือกตั้งใหม่ กทม.ยังมากสุด 33 คน |url=https://workpointtoday.com/politics-electorate/ |access-date=23 October 2022 |website=workpointTODAY |language=th |archive-date=14 January 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230114211905/https://workpointtoday.com/politics-electorate/ |url-status=live }}</ref>

The Election Commission had just finished drawing electoral boundaries in February 2023—just three months before the election, while many candidates had already begun campaigning. The original drawing was challenged in the Constitutional Court as the Commission included non-citizens in the calculation. The Court struck down the drawing, prompting the Commission to revise and present another boundary drawing. The second drawing was also challenged in the administrative courts amid the accusation of [[gerrymandering]] by the Commission, but the court eventually ruled the drawing was legal in April 2023.<ref>{{cite news |title=ศาลปกครองสูงสุด 'ยกฟ้อง' คดี 'อรรถวิชช์' ฟ้อง กกต. แบ่งเขตเลือกตั้ง ชี้ ชอบด้วยกฎหมาย |url=https://prachatai.com/journal/2023/04/103540 |access-date=10 April 2023 |work=Prachatai |language=th |archive-date=10 April 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230410153640/https://prachatai.com/journal/2023/04/103540 |url-status=live }}</ref>

==Leading up to the election==
Since late 2022, ex-politician [[Chuwit Kamolvisit]] began attacking Prayut whose relative was allegedly linked to Chinese nationals involving drug, gambling and money laundering.<ref>{{cite web|title=China's triads and corrupt Thai cops undermined national security|date=13 March 2023|work=Thai PBS World|url=https://www.thaipbsworld.com/chinas-triads-and-corrupt-thai-cops-undermined-national-security/|access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Ex-junta leader morphs into political party member|date=10 January 2023|work=Prachatai English|url=https://prachataienglish.com/node/10176|access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref> He later turned to Bhumjai Thai Party, accusing it of corruption regarding [[Orange Line (Bangkok)|Orange Line]] rapid transit planning and promoting unrestricted use of marijuana.<ref>{{cite web|first=Joseph|last=O' Connor|title=Anutin rejects Chuwit's claims about Bangkok's mass transit Orange line and Bhumjai Thai's election odds|date=25 February 2023|url=https://www.thaiexaminer.com/thai-news-foreigners/2023/02/25/anutin-plays-down-chuwit-corruption-claims-orange-line-bangkok/|work=Thai Examiner|access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref>

In January 2023, two youth political activists began a hunger strike calling for the release of political prisoners detained before trial. Many of them were charged with lèse-majesté, which led to another debate on amending or cancellation of the law. Political parties were asked for their stance on the issue.<ref>{{cite web|first=Kaweewit|last=Kaewjinda|title=Thai women activists end hunger strike, vow to keep up fight|date=March 11, 2023|publisher=The Associated Press|url=https://apnews.com/article/thailand-hunger-strike-monarchy-protesters-9d3cb002fc034a8f79ccdd10f667b18a|access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref>

Beginning in January 2023, many civil society organizations called for volunteers to keep their eyes on the election process, citing the untrustworthiness of the Election Commission and its decision not to report the election result in real-time.<ref>{{cite news|title=100,000 volunteers to monitor vote count in upcoming election|date=2 March 2023|work=The Bangkok Post|url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2518176/100-000-volunteers-to-monitor-vote-count-in-upcoming-election|access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref> As of March 2023, the movement had grown to 50 organizations, including media, who declared they would report the unofficial election result when the Commission would not.<ref>{{cite web|title=Media Outlets Form Alliance on Election Results Coverage|date=27 March 2023|publisher=National News Bureau of Thailand|url=https://thainews.prd.go.th/en/news/detail/TCATG230327104419006|access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref>

On 20 March 2023, the House of Representatives was dissolved—three days before the term ended. Due to a legal technicality, the election date was postponed for another week from 7 May 2023. The move also allowed politicians more time to switch parties, which many analysts interpreted as the real motive behind it. Some commentators also noticed that the election day was set during the period of university exams and after a long holiday which could make voting inconvenient for a lot of Thais who have to return to vote in their constituencies.<ref>{{cite news |title=The 2023 General Election in Thailand: Possible Scenarios {{!}} Heinrich Böll Stiftung |url=https://www.boell.de/en/2023/05/09/2023-general-election-thailand-possible-scenarios |access-date=9 May 2023 |work=Heinrich-Böll-Stiftung |language=en |archive-date=9 May 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230509170212/https://www.boell.de/en/2023/05/09/2023-general-election-thailand-possible-scenarios |url-status=live }}</ref>

In March 2023, a group of hackers posted the personal data of 55 million Thais for sale online. The group called 9Near with a symbol resembling that of the Move Forward Party, also mentioned on their page: "Almost election, choose wisely."<ref>{{cite news |title=GOV'T BLOCKS WEBSITE THAT THREATENS TO LEAK PERSONAL DATA OF 55 MILLION THAIS |url=https://www.khaosodenglish.com/news/crimecourtscalamity/2023/04/01/govt-blocks-website-that-threatens-to-leak-personal-data-of-55-million-thais/ |access-date=1 April 2023 |work=Khaosod English |archive-date=1 April 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230401080410/https://www.khaosodenglish.com/news/crimecourtscalamity/2023/04/01/govt-blocks-website-that-threatens-to-leak-personal-data-of-55-million-thais/ |url-status=live }}</ref>

In May, ex-prime minister [[Thaksin Shinawatra]] tweeted that he was ready to return to Thailand before July 2023 and "enter the legal process". He faced possible prison time from prior convictions.<ref>{{cite news |title=Thaksin wants to come home, 'enter legal process' |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2566509/thaksin-wants-to-come-home-enter-legal-process |access-date=9 May 2023 |work=Bangkok Post |language=en}}</ref>

The Thai economy was still recovering from the COVID-19 pandemic, but it was still struggling with multiple issues, including high energy and electricity costs, the number of employers still fewer than before the pandemic, rising household debts, and falling income growth. Other economic issues such as minimum wage, farm subsidies, and welfare were considered top voting issues. Most political parties campaigned on subsidies and tax exemptions.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Chandran |first1=Nyshka |title=Thailand election: Campaign freebies may be a 'band-aid solution' with fiscal risks |url=https://www.cnbc.com/2023/05/09/thai-polls-economic-populism-takes-center-stage-in-upcoming-election.html |access-date=9 May 2023 |work=CNBC |language=en |archive-date=9 May 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230509021326/https://www.cnbc.com/2023/05/09/thai-polls-economic-populism-takes-center-stage-in-upcoming-election.html |url-status=live }}</ref>

==Overseas and advanced voting==
More than 2 million Thais registered for advance voting, while other 100,000 people registered to vote from overseas.<ref>{{cite news |title=Advance voters queue nationwide as temperatures soar |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2565159/advance-voters-queue-nationwide-as-temperatures-soar |access-date=9 May 2023 |work=Bangkok Post |language=en}}</ref> Thailand's Ministry of Foreign Affairs reported that around 80% of the eligible overseas voters had done so.<ref>{{cite news |title=Overseas voting almost done |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2563511/overseas-voting-almost-done |access-date=9 May 2023 |work=Bangkok Post |language=en}}</ref> Even with high temperatures, some districts still expected a 90% turnout.<ref>{{cite news |title=Big turnout at polling stations for advance voting in Thailand's general election |url=https://www.thaipbsworld.com/big-turnout-at-polling-stations-for-advance-voting-in-thailands-general-election/ |access-date=9 May 2023 |work=Thai PBS World |archive-date=9 May 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230509163424/https://www.thaipbsworld.com/big-turnout-at-polling-stations-for-advance-voting-in-thailands-general-election/ |url-status=live }}</ref>

The Election Commission's conduct of advance voting drew sharp criticism from the public, especially from numerous cases of incorrect information on ballot envelops. Chuvit Kamolvisit also pointed out irregularities in some Northeastern provinces where unusually high number of voters voted in advance and there were reports of collection of ID cards. More than a million people also signed an [[online petition]] to oust the Election Commission.<ref>{{cite news |title=EC vows poll will be error-free |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2566005/ec-vows-poll-will-be-error-free |access-date=9 May 2023 |work=Bangkok Post |language=en}}</ref>

[[File:2023 Thai general election result by camps.svg|thumb|right|350px|Total seats of pro/anti-junta parties]] 

The [[Move Forward Party]] won the most seats with 151, including sweeping all but one of Bangkok's seats, outperforming another opposition party, [[Pheu Thai Party|Pheu Thai]], which was considered the frontrunner.<ref name=":1" /> Analysts saw this as a significant political shift, and Move Forward supporters called the results a "wind of change" and the "dawn of a new era".<ref>{{cite news |date=14 May 2023 |title=Thailand elections: Voters deliver stunning win for reform |work=BBC News |url=https://www.bbc.com/news/world-asia-65567781 |access-date=15 May 2023}}</ref> This also broke Pheu Thai's run of being the party with the most MPs since 2001 (including its predecessors, the [[People's Power Party (Thailand)|People's Power Party]] and [[Thai Rak Thai Party]]).<ref>{{cite news |last1=Ratcliffe |first1=Rebecca |date=15 May 2023 |title=Thailand election 2023: opposition delivers crushing blow to military rule |work=The Guardian |url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2023/may/15/thailand-election-2023-national-elections-opposition-parties-crushing-blow-military-rule-junta |url-status=live |access-date=15 May 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230514234106/https://www.theguardian.com/world/2023/may/15/thailand-election-2023-national-elections-opposition-parties-crushing-blow-military-rule-junta |archive-date=14 May 2023}}</ref>

The two main parties connected to the former [[National Council for Peace and Order|military junta]], the [[Palang Pracharath Party]] and its split-off [[United Thai Nation Party]]<ref>{{cite web |last1=Wongcha-um |first1=Panu |last2=Thepgumpanat |first2=Panarat |date=15 May 2023 |title=Thailand opposition crushes military parties in election rout |url=https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/old-rivalries-new-battle-thailand-goes-polls-2023-05-13/ |access-date=16 May 2023 |publisher=Reuters}}</ref> lost half their seats compared to Palang Pracharath's result in 2019. The [[Democrat Party (Thailand)|Democrat Party]], a junior coalition partner, also lost over half its seats. Bhumjai Thai was the only coalition party which gained seats in this election.

Analysts were surprised at Move Forward's performance. It was attributed to a desire for change, dissatisfaction of the Prayuth premiership, and a reluctance to vote for the formerly dominant Pheu Thai. Move Forward's distinctive commitment to reforming the country's [[Lèse-majesté in Thailand|lèse-majesté law]] could also have been an issue that influenced voters.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Ratcliffe |first1=Rebecca |date=15 May 2023 |title=Thai election may be turning point as taboo over monarchy fades |work=The Guardian |url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2023/may/15/thai-election-may-be-turning-point-as-taboo-over-monarchy-fades |access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref> Napon Jatusripitak, a Thai political scientist at the [[ISEAS–Yusof Ishak Institute]], opined that this election indexed a readiness in the country to transcend past political divides and to instead focus on structural reforms "concerning the role of traditional power centers in Thailand".<ref name="DW">{{cite news |title=Thai election: Big win for opposition, but uncertainty looms |language=en |work=DW |url=https://www.dw.com/en/thai-election-big-win-for-opposition-but-uncertainty-looms/a-65628441 |access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref>

==Government formation==
===<span class="anchor" id="Move Forward-led coalition"></span>Move Forward–led coalition===
Move Forward leader [[Pita Limjaroenrat]] announced a six-party alliance with four former opposition parties—Pheu Thai, [[Thai Sang Thai Party|Thai Sang Thai]], [[Prachachart Party|Prachachat]] and the [[Thai Liberal Party]]—and one new party, the Fair Party.<ref>{{Cite news |last1=Setboonsarng |first1=Chayut |last2=Wongcha-um |first2=Panu |date=15 May 2023 |title=Thai opposition parties agree to coalition after election success |language=en |work=Reuters |url=https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/deals-be-done-thai-opposition-parties-look-form-government-2023-05-15/ |access-date=15 May 2023}}</ref><ref name=bkkpost_15052023>{{cite news|title=Pita declares six-party coalition with 309 MPs|date=15 May 2023|work=Bangkok Post|url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2571024/pita-declares-six-party-coalition-with-309-mps|access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref> The coalition would include 310 MPs,<ref name="auto">{{cite news|title=More senators back Pita's bid to be prime minister|date=19 May 2023|work=English|url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2573517/more-senators-back-pitas-bid-to-be-prime-minister|access-date=20 May 2023}}</ref> more than enough for a majority government in the House of Representatives.<ref>{{cite web|first=Kiki|last=Siregar|title=FAQ: What is the state of play in Thailand as Move Forward attempts to form the government?|work=Channel News Asia|url=https://www.channelnewsasia.com/asia/thailand-election-2023-pita-limjaroenrat-politics-state-play-faq-3489371|access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref> It later grew to eight parties (with Pheu Thai Ruam Palang and the New Social Power Party joining), accounting to 313 MPs.<ref>{{cite news |title=Pita announces 8-party coalition plan |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2573332/pita-announces-8-party-coalition-plan |access-date=24 May 2023 |work=Bangkok Post |language=en}}</ref> On 22 May, the eight parties signed a historic [[memorandum of understanding]] listing 23 policies they agreed on, including restoration of democracy, enacting marriage equality law, and reformation of the security and justice system. However, the issue of lèse-majesté was left out.<ref>{{cite news |title=Thailand Opposition Signs Coalition Agreement, Pledges to Restore Democracy |url=https://www.voanews.com/a/thailand-opposition-signs-coalition-agreement-pledges-to-restore-democracy-/7104063.html |access-date=24 May 2023 |work=VOA |date=22 May 2023 |language=en}}</ref>

Pita needed 376 votes from the complete [[National Assembly (Thailand)|National Assembly]] to be voted prime minister.<ref name=bkkpost_15052023/> Due to the foreseeable gridlock that no senators would vote for a Pita premiership, some started to see Bhumjai Thai, which finished third in the election, a "kingmaker" and allying itself with Move Forward–led coalition, but during campaigning its leader Anutin had ruled out an alliance with parties that seek to reform the lèse-majesté law.<ref>{{cite magazine |title=The Voting in Thailand's Election Is Finished, But the Politicking Is Just Beginning|url=https://time.com/6279758/thailand-elections-move-forward-party-pheu-thai/ |access-date=3 June 2023 |magazine=Time|first=Charlie|last=Campbell |date=15 May 2023 |language=en}}</ref> Some senators said that they would not automatically vote for Pita, while stating that they had the country's best interests at heart;<ref>{{cite news |title=Senators: PM votes unbiased and in country's best interest |language=en |work=Bangkok Post |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2569862/senators-pm-votes-unbiased-and-in-countrys-best-interest |access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref> however, 15 senators were reportedly gearing to back his bid to be prime minister.<ref name="auto"/> Civil society also began pressuring senators to vote for Pita,<ref>{{cite news |title=Thai senators come under pressure over PM vote |work=Thai PBS World |url=https://www.thaipbsworld.com/thai-senators-come-under-pressure-over-pm-vote/ |access-date=16 May 2023}}</ref> and, on 23 May, organized a rally near the parliament.<ref>{{cite news |title=Move Forward supporters rally to persuade senators to vote for Pita |url=https://www.thaipbsworld.com/move-forward-supporters-rally-to-persuade-senators-to-vote-for-pita/ |access-date=24 May 2023 |work=Thai PBS World}}</ref>

Apart from the full parliament prime minister vote, there was much anxiety of a possible dissolution of the Move Forward party or court rulings against it — the same fate as its predecessor, the Future Forward party. Pita was also accused of owning shares in [[ITV (Thailand)|ITV]], a de-listed television broadcaster since 2008, which could disqualify him from being an MP.<ref name=DW/> After the election, there was a conflict between Move Forward and Pheu Thai over the House speaker seat.<ref>{{cite news |title=Pheu Thai claims House speaker's position |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2577580/pheu-thai-claims-house-speakers-position |access-date=29 May 2023 |work=Bangkok Post |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |title=Pita Urges Unity After Thai Coalition Squabbles Over House Speaker Job |url=https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-05-26/pita-urges-unity-after-thai-coalition-squabbles-over-house-speaker-job#xj4y7vzkg |access-date=29 May 2023 |work=Bloomberg|first=Patpicha|last=Tanakasempipat |date=26 May 2023 |language=en}}</ref> Some self-proclaimed Pheu Thai supporters also urged their party to leave the Move Forward–led coalition.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Connor |first1=Mitch |title=Pheu Thai supporters urge split from MFP amid House speaker rift |url=https://thethaiger.com/news/national/pheu-thai-supporters-urge-split-from-mfp-amid-house-speaker-rift |access-date=29 May 2023 |work=Thaiger |date=29 May 2023 |language=en}}</ref> Rumors that Pheu Thai could join the old coalition parties from before the election persisted.<ref>{{cite news |title=Thaksin 'flabbergasted' by Chuvit's secret-coalition claim |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2580670/thaksin-flabbergasted-by-chuvits-secret-coalition-claim |access-date=29 May 2023 |work=Bangkok Post |language=en}}</ref> The [[Royal Thai Armed Forces]] pledged to respect the results of the general election and not launch a coup.<ref>{{Cite news |date=2023-05-12 |title=Thai Military Rules Out Coup After Election as Campaigning Ends |language=en |work=Bloomberg.com |url=https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-05-12/thai-military-rules-out-coup-after-election-as-campaigning-ends |access-date=2023-05-17}}</ref>

On 3 July, the new parliament was opened by [[Vajiralongkorn|King Rama X]], and the coalition agreed to [[Wan Muhamad Noor Matha]] of the [[Prachachat Party]] to be nominated as speaker, paving the way for government formation. Wan previously served as Speaker from 1996 to 2000.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2604481/move-forward-pheu-thai-clinch-house-speaker-compromise|title=Move Forward, Pheu Thai clinch House speaker compromise|newspaper=Bangkok Post }}</ref>

On 13 July, the government formation was blocked, as Pita's coalition failed to gain the requisite 375 votes in parliament. While 324 members of parliament voted in favor, 182 voted against and 199 abstained.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2023/jul/13/winning-thailand-candidate-for-pm-blocked-from-power-pita-limjaroenrat|title=Thailand's winning candidate for PM blocked from power|first1=Rebecca|last1=Ratcliffe|first2=Navaon|last2=Siradapuvadol|work=[[The Guardian]]|location=[[Bangkok]]|date=13 July 2023|access-date=13 July 2023}}</ref> Most of the [[Senate of Thailand|junta-appointed senators]] refused to endorse Pita for [[Prime Minister of Thailand|Prime Minister]], leading to protest and criticism.<ref>{{Cite magazine |date=2023-07-13 |title=Thai Parliament Blocks Pita in First Round of Prime Minister Selection |url=https://time.com/6294351/thailand-prime-minister-parliament-vote-pita/ |access-date=2023-07-13 |magazine=Time |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |last1=Ratcliffe |first1=Rebecca |last2=Siradapuvadol |first2=Navaon |date=2023-07-13 |title=Thailand's winning candidate for PM blocked from power |language=en-GB |work=The Guardian |url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2023/jul/13/winning-thailand-candidate-for-pm-blocked-from-power-pita-limjaroenrat |access-date=2023-07-13 |issn=0261-3077}}</ref>

On 19 July, the National Assembly convened for a second time to elect a new prime minister.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Olarn |first=Helen Regan,Kocha |date=2023-07-13 |title=Critical moment for Thai democracy as parliament fails to elect new prime minister |url=https://www.cnn.com/2023/07/12/asia/thailand-parliament-prime-ministerial-vote-intl-hnk/index.html |access-date=2023-07-19 |website=CNN |language=en}}</ref> That day, the Constitutional Court unanimously voted to accept a case against Pita regarding his inherited shares of the defunct iTV. They also voted 7–2 to suspend him from MP duty until it reached a ruling, though this did not preclude him from being nominated for prime minister.<ref>{{cite news|title=Charter court suspends Pita|date=19 July 2023|work=Bangkok Post|url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2614193/charter-court-suspends-pita|access-date=19 July 2023}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |last=Ives |first=Mike |date=2023-07-19 |title=An Anxious Thailand Awaits a New Leader: Here's What to Know |language=en-US |work=The New York Times |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2023/07/19/world/asia/pita-limjaroenrat-prime-minister-thailand.html |access-date=2023-07-19 |issn=0362-4331}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |date=2023-07-19 |title=Thai court suspends prime minister hopeful Pita's lawmaker status after his reformist party swept elections |url=https://news.yahoo.com/thai-court-suspends-prime-ministerial-052225282.html |access-date=2023-07-19 |website=Yahoo News |language=en-US}}</ref> In the subsequent debate, the National Assembly voted against considering him for another round of voting for prime minister.<ref name="auto1">{{Cite news |date=2023-07-19 |title=Thai Parliament Bars Election Winner From Running for PM Post |language=en |work=Bloomberg.com |url=https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-07-19/thai-parliament-bars-election-winner-from-running-for-pm-post |access-date=2023-07-19}}</ref> His renomination was argued to have been against a parliamentary rule prohibiting a repeat motion.<ref>{{cite news|first1=Mike|last1=Ives|first2=Muktita|last2=Suhartono|title=Protesters Gather in Thailand as Parliament Faces Gridlock|date=19 July 2023|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2023/07/19/world/asia/pita-limjaroenrat-prime-minister-thailand.html|access-date=20 July 2023}}</ref><ref name=bp_19072023>{{cite news|title=Parliament rejects Pita's renomination for PM|date=19 July 2023|work=The Bangkok Post|url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2614294/parliament-rejects-pitas-renomination-for-pm|access-date=20 July 2023}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Thai parliament rejects Pita's PM nomination |url=https://www.dw.com/en/thai-parliament-rejects-pitas-pm-nomination/a-66280247 |access-date=2023-07-19 |website=dw.com |language=en}}</ref><ref name="auto1"/> In the voting for his renomination, 312 voters supported, 394 rejected—most of which were senators—eight abstained and one did not vote.<ref name=bp_19072023/> The decision to prohibit renominations was controversial, with critics arguing that it was unconstitutional. Many complaints were filed to the [[Ombudsmen (Thailand)|Office of the Ombudsman]], which on 24 July agreed to forward the case to the Constitutional Court.<ref>{{Cite news |title=Parliament vote for PM postponed indefinitely |work=Bangkok Post |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2617559/parliament-vote-for-pm-postponed-indefinitely |access-date=2023-07-25}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |title=Ombudsman asks court to postpone PM vote |work=Bangkok Post |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2616894/ombudsman-asks-court-to-postpone-pm-vote |access-date=2023-07-24}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Rejection of Pita's renomination as PM 'unconstitutional' : Ombudsman |url=https://www.thaipbsworld.com/thai-ombudsman-asks-the-charter-court-to-suspend-pm-selection-process/ |access-date=2023-07-24 |website=Thai PBS World |date=24 July 2023 |language=en-US}}</ref> This led to further prime ministerial voting being postponed by Parliament; the next vote had originally been scheduled for 27 July.

===<span class="anchor" id="Pheu Thai-led coalition"></span>Pheu Thai–led coalition===
After Move Forward's attempts were blocked, the opportunity to lead the government formation was passed on to Pheu Thai, who were expected to nominate [[Srettha Thavisin]] to be prime minister.<ref>{{Cite news |date=2023-07-19 |title=Thai Parliament to Vote for PM Again on July 27 |language=en |work=Bloomberg.com |url=https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-07-19/thai-parliament-to-vote-for-pm-again-on-july-27-house-speaker |access-date=2023-07-21}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |title=Pheu Thai to seek support from Senate, other parties |work=Bangkok Post |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2615487/pheu-thai-to-seek-support-from-senate-other-parties |access-date=2023-07-21}}</ref> As part of Pheu Thai's attempts to form a government, talks were held with many of the conservative and/or pro-junta parties, who insisted that they would not support any coalition including Move Forward due to its policy on amending the [[Lèse-majesté in Thailand|lèse-majesté laws]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=Thailand's Pheu Thai courts conservative parties |url=https://asia.nikkei.com/Politics/Thai-election/Thailand-s-Pheu-Thai-courts-conservative-parties |access-date=2023-07-29 |website=Nikkei Asia |language=en-GB}}</ref> This, coupled with an announcement that exiled former prime minister [[Thaksin Shinawatra]] would return to Thailand, fueled speculations of a deal with outgoing coalition parties that would see Pheu Thai abandoning the coalition with Move Forward.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Parpart |first=Erich |date=2023-07-27 |title=Leaders' Meeting in Hong Kong Sparks Speculation on Government Formation and Thaksin's Return to Thailand - Thai Enquirer Current Affairs |url=https://www.thaienquirer.com/50301/leaders-meeting-in-hong-kong-sparks-speculation-on-government-formation-and-thaksins-return-to-thailand/ |access-date=2023-07-29 |website=Thai Enquirer |language=en-US}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Thaksin's pending return signals done deal? |url=https://www.thaipbsworld.com/thaksins-pending-return-signals-done-deal/ |access-date=2023-07-29 |website=Thai PBS World |date=28 July 2023 |language=en-US}}</ref> 

On 2 August, Pheu Thai announced that it was leaving the eight-party coalition and would attempt to form a new coalition that excludes Move Forward, with Srettha as the candidate for prime minister. On 3 August, the Constitutional Court announced that its decision on Pita's renomination would be made on 16 August. In response, the PM vote, scheduled for 4 August, was again postponed to 22 August.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Thepgumpanat |first=Panarat |date=2023-08-03 |title=Vote for Thai PM postponed, uncertainty prolonged |language=en |work=Reuters |url=https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thai-alliance-reaffirms-backing-pitas-pm-bid-2023-07-17/ |access-date=2023-08-03}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |title=Vote for PM postponed |work=Bangkok Post |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2622895/vote-for-pm-postponed |access-date=2023-08-03}}</ref> 

On 7 August, Pheu Thai and Bhumjaithai announced an alliance that would try to form a new coalition government.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Thailand's Pheu Thai forms new coalition with third-largest party |url=https://asia.nikkei.com/Politics/Thai-election/Thailand-s-Pheu-Thai-forms-new-coalition-with-third-largest-party |access-date=2023-08-07 |website=Nikkei Asia |language=en-GB}}</ref> They were joined by six parties—Prachachart, Pheu Thai Ruam Palang, Chart Pattana Kla, Thai Liberal, New Social Power, and the Party of Thai Counties—on 9 August, followed by Chart Thai Pattana on 10 August, bringing their total to 238 MPs, which is less than the initial opposition coalition but which was seen as more favorable to the conservative military establishment in the Senate.<ref>{{Cite news |date=2023-08-09 |title=Pheu Thai brings 6 small parties into its alliance |work=Bangkok Post |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2626294/pheu-thai-brings-6-small-parties-into-its-alliance |access-date=2023-08-09}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Thailand's Pheu Thai Party Welcomes New Parties Into Potential Governing Coalition |url=https://thediplomat.com/2023/08/thailands-pheu-thai-party-welcomes-new-parties-into-potential-governing-coalition/ |access-date=2023-08-10 |website=The Diplomat |language=en-US}}</ref> 

On 16 August, the Constitutional Court dismissed the petitions on Pita's renomination, stating that the plaintiff was not the directly affected party, clearing the way for a new round of prime ministerial voting.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Parpart |first=Erich |date=2023-08-16 |title=Constitutional Court dismisses Move Forward Party's petition, clearing path for the next prime ministerial voting - Thai Enquirer Current Affairs |url=https://www.thaienquirer.com/50433/constitutional-court-dismisses-move-forward-partys-petition-clearing-path-for-the-next-prime-ministerial-voting/ |access-date=2023-08-16 |website=Thai Enquirer |language=en-US}}</ref> An eleven-party coalition was announced on 21 August, including the aforementioned parties along with the military-associated parties United Thai Nation and Palang Pracharath for a total of 314 MPs.<ref name=":3" /> Pheu Thai's alliance with the military parties was controversial among many voters, including some of its own supporters, as its leaders had promised not to join with them before the election.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Ratcliffe |first=Rebecca |date=2023-08-21 |title=Thai party of Thaksin Shinawatra strikes deal with ex-military rivals |language=en-GB |work=The Guardian |url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2023/aug/21/pheu-thai-party-thaksin-shinawatra-deal-ex-military-rivals |access-date=2023-08-21 |issn=0261-3077}}</ref> 

A poll published by [[National Institute of Development Administration|NIDA]] the day prior showed that around 64% of those surveyed opposed Pheu Thai allying with military-backed parties.<ref name=":3">{{Cite web |title=Thailand's Pheu Thai allies with military rivals to form new government |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2023/8/21/thailands-pheu-thai-to-ally-with-military-rivals-to-form-new-government |access-date=2023-08-21 |website=www.aljazeera.com |language=en}}</ref> On 22 August at 9{{Nbsp}}am Bangkok time, Thaksin returned to Thailand and landed at [[Don Mueang International Airport]], following 15 years of self-imposed exile, and was promptly sent to court and then prison to begin serving an eight-year sentence for his outstanding corruption convictions.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Ratcliffe |first1=Rebecca |last2=Siradapuvadol |first2=Navaon |title=Thaksin Shinawatra jailed on return to Thailand as his party regains power |url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2023/aug/22/thaksin-shinawatra-anger-and-anticipation-in-thailand-as-exiled-former-pm-expected-to-return |access-date=22 August 2023 |work=The Guardian |date=22 August 2023}}</ref> Hours later, he was transferred to the [[Police General Hospital]], which prompted accusations of special treatment.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2023-08-29 |title=Daughter of ex-Thai leader Thaksin says he is fatigued, as criticism grows of his hospitalization |url=https://apnews.com/article/thailand-thaksin-shinawatra-hospital-paetongtarn-8e866a52fede6554112955275225dfa0 |access-date=2023-08-31 |website=AP News |language=en}}</ref> On 31 August, outgoing Justice Minister [[Wissanu Krea-ngam]] confirmed that Thaksin had requested a royal pardon,<ref>{{Cite news |date=2023-08-31 |title=Thailand's billionaire ex-PM Thaksin submits royal pardon request - media |language=en |work=Reuters |url=https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thailands-ex-pm-thaksin-has-submitted-request-royal-pardon-media-2023-08-31/ |access-date=2023-08-31}}</ref> and on 1 September the [[Royal Gazette (Thailand)|Royal Gazette]] announced the King had commuted his sentence to one year.<ref>{{Cite news |date=2023-09-01 |title=Thai king reduces former PM Thaksin's prison sentence to one year |language=en |work=Reuters |url=https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thai-king-reduces-former-pm-thaksins-prison-sentence-one-year-2023-09-01/ |access-date=2023-09-01}}</ref>

On the evening of 22 August, the day of Thaksin's arrival, Srettha Thavisin was elected with 482 votes for, 165 votes against, with 81 abstentions.<ref>{{Cite news |title=Parliament elects Srettha prime minister |language=en |work=Bangkok Post |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2633946/parliament-elects-srettha-prime-minister |access-date=2023-08-22}}</ref> On 23 August, the King formally endorsed Srettha to become the 30th Prime Minister of Thailand.<ref>{{Cite news |date=23 August 2023 |title=Srettha endorsed by king as Thailand's new PM – official |language=en |work=Reuters |url=https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/srettha-endorsed-by-king-thailands-new-pm-official-2023-08-23/ |access-date=23 August 2023 |archive-date=23 August 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230823135310/https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/srettha-endorsed-by-king-thailands-new-pm-official-2023-08-23/ |url-status=live }}</ref> The endorsement document was posted on the Royal Gazette's website.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Ratchakitcha |title=พระบรมราชโองการ ประกาศ แต่งตั้งนายกรัฐมนตรี [นายเศรษฐา ทวีสิน] |url=https://ratchakitcha.soc.go.th/documents/140D201S0000000000100.pdf |url-status=live |access-date=23 August 2023 |archive-date=23 August 2023 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20230823132500/https://ratchakitcha.soc.go.th/documents/140D201S0000000000100.pdf }}</ref> At the event, Srettha also gave his first speech as prime minister.<ref>{{Citation |title="เศรษฐา ทวีสิน" รับสนองพระบรมราชโองการ แต่งตั้ง เป็นนายกฯคนที่ 30 ของไทยแล้ว | date=23 August 2023 |url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IzoK_p279kg |access-date=23 cabinet|His cabinet]] was sworn in on 5 September.<ref>{{Cite news |date=2023-09-05 |title=Thailand's king swears in new PM and cabinet |language=en |work=Reuters |url=https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/thailands-king-swears-new-pm-cabinet-2023-09-05/ |access-date=2023-09-08}}</ref>

The protracted government formation led to a further issue: Move Forward had now become the largest opposition party, but one of its MPs, [[Padipat Suntiphada]], had already been elected deputy house speaker by the eight-party coalition. Constitutionally, the opposition leader could not be from the same party as a speaker or deputy speaker. As a result, Move Forward strategically expelled Padipat so that the leadership of the opposition could be taken by its new leader [[Chaithawat Tulathon]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=In a tactical move, Move Forward party sacks Padipat |url=https://www.thaipbsworld.com/in-a-tactical-move-move-forward-party-sacks-padipat/ |access-date=2023-10-01 |website=Thai PBS World |date=29 September 2023 |language=en-US}}</ref> Government parties criticized the expulsion as an undignified political gambit that would in practice keep both positions in Move Forward's control,<ref>{{Cite web |title=Move Forward's expulsion of Padipat slammed as shameful |url=https://www.thaipbsworld.com/move-forwards-expulsion-of-padipat-slammed-as-shameful/ |access-date=2023-10-01 |website=Thai PBS World |date=29 September 2023 |language=en-US}}</ref> while Padipat insisted he would not act as Move Forward's nominee.<ref>{{Cite news |title=Padipat shrugs off criticism over 'expulsion' |language=en |work=Bangkok Post |url=https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/politics/2654871/padipat-shrugs-off-criticism-over-expulsion |access-date=2023-10-01}}</ref>
