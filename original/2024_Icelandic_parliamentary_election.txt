Parliamentary elections were held in [[Iceland]] on 30 November 2024 to elect the 63 members of the [[Althing]]. The [[Centre-left politics|centre-left]] [[Social Democratic Alliance]], led by [[Kristrún Frostadóttir]], outperformed the ruling [[Independence Party (Iceland)|Independence Party]] to win the most seats, at 15.<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://apnews.com/article/iceland-election-results-social-democrats-989773ec8a24cddef7ba78d5aa933df2 |title=Social democrats gain and incumbents are punished in Iceland’s election |date=1 December 2024 |access-date=1 December 2024 |website=Associated Press |language=en |archive-date=1 December 2024 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20241201150818/https://apnews.com/article/iceland-election-results-social-democrats-989773ec8a24cddef7ba78d5aa933df2 |url-status=live }}</ref> The election saw the worst performance by the [[Independence Party (Iceland)|Independence Party]], [[Progressive Party (Iceland)|Progressive Party]], the [[Left-Green Movement]], and the [[Pirate Party (Iceland)|Pirate Party]] in each of the parties' histories, while [[Viðreisn]], the [[People's Party (Iceland)|People's Party]], and the [[Centre Party (Iceland)|Centre Party]] saw their best performance in each of the parties' histories. This follows a trend of Icelanders voting against every post–[[Great Recession|2008 recession]] government except during the [[2021 Icelandic parliamentary election|2021 election]].<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.france24.com/en/live-news/20241201-social-democrats-set-to-overtake-ruling-party-in-iceland-snap-election |title=Social Democrats overtake PM's party in Iceland vote |date=1 December 2024 |access-date=1 December 2024 |website=France 24 |language=en |archive-date=1 December 2024 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20241201121640/https://www.france24.com/en/live-news/20241201-social-democrats-set-to-overtake-ruling-party-in-iceland-snap-election |url-status=live }}</ref>

The Left-Green Movement and the Pirate Party lost all of their parliamentary representation, with each failing to obtain a seat for the first time since their foundation in the [[1999 Icelandic parliamentary election|1999]] and [[2013 Icelandic parliamentary election|2013 elections]], respectively.<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2024/12/1/social-democrats-win-icelands-snap-election-as-incumbents-punished |title=Social Democrats win Iceland’s snap elections as incumbents punished |date=1 December 2024 |access-date=2 December 2024 |website=Al Jazeera |language=en}}</ref>

==Background==
===Previous election===
{{main|2021 Icelandic parliamentary election}}
The [[2021 Icelandic parliamentary election|2021 parliamentary election]] took place on 25 September, a month before the latest date allowed by law. The government coalition of the [[Left-Green Movement]], the [[Progressive Party (Iceland)|Progressive Party]] and the [[Independence Party (Iceland)|Independence Party]], led by prime minister [[Katrín Jakobsdóttir]], had been in place since the [[2017 Icelandic parliamentary election|2017 elections]]. This unusual coalition of parties from the left wing, the center and the right wing of Icelandic mainstream politics maintained its majority in parliament in the elections as the Independence Party had the same number of seats as before while the Progressive Party added 5 seats and the Left-Green Movement lost 3. As for the opposition parties, the [[People's Party (Iceland)|People's Party]] gained two seats and [[Viðreisn]] gained 1 seat. The Pirate Party held steady but the [[Social Democratic Alliance]] lost 1 seat and the [[Centre Party (Iceland)|Centre Party]] lost 4 of its seats in parliament. The [[Icelandic Socialist Party]] received 4.1% of the vote but no seats as 5% of the national vote is needed to be eligible for the allocation of [[leveling seat]]s. 

The coalition partners from the previous term soon decided that the first option for coalition talks should be to renew their coalition. The [[second cabinet of Katrín Jakobsdóttir]] was then formed on 28 November.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.reuters.com/world/europe/icelands-left-right-coalition-agrees-take-another-term-2021-11-28/|title=Iceland's left-right coalition agrees to take another term|work=Reuters|date=28 November 2021|access-date=3 December 2021|archive-date=30 November 2021|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20211130052716/https://www.reuters.com/world/europe/icelands-left-right-coalition-agrees-take-another-term-2021-11-28/|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |title=Katrin Jakobsdottir, Iceland's Staunch Feminist PM, Begins Second Term |url=https://www.voanews.com/a/katrin-jakobsdottir-iceland-staunch-feminist-pm-begins-second-term/6331139.html |date=28 November 2021 |work=[[Voice of America]] |access-date=15 December 2021 |archive-date=29 November 2021 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20211129092320/https://www.voanews.com/a/katrin-jakobsdottir-iceland-staunch-feminist-pm-begins-second-term/6331139.html |url-status=live }}</ref>

===Fall of government===
In April 2024, Katrín Jakobsdóttir resigned to contest the [[2024 Icelandic presidential election]], which she lost. She was succeeded as prime minister by [[Bjarni Benediktsson (born 1970)|Bjarni Benediktsson]].<ref name="collapse">{{cite news |date=13 October 2024 |title=Iceland PM calls new election as coalition government collapses |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2024/10/13/iceland-pm-calls-new-election-as-coalition-government-collapses |access-date=14 October 2024 |work=Al Jazeera |archive-date=10 November 2024 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20241110003614/https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2024/10/13/iceland-pm-calls-new-election-as-coalition-government-collapses |url-status=live }}</ref>

On 13 October, prime minister Bjarni Benediktsson announced that the government had collapsed due to disputes over foreign policy, asylum seekers, and energy, prompting him to call for new elections and ask President [[Halla Tómasdóttir]] the next day to dissolve the [[Althingi]] and set the elections for 30 November.<ref name="collapse"/><ref>{{cite news |url=https://apnews.com/article/iceland-snap-election-coalition-government-9d170f37de3228506de93444668c416a |title=Iceland appears headed for a snap election after governing coalition collapses |work=[[Associated Press]] |date=14 October 2024 |access-date=14 October 2024 |archive-date=10 November 2024 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20241110003618/https://apnews.com/article/iceland-snap-election-coalition-government-9d170f37de3228506de93444668c416a |url-status=live }}</ref> In particular, Bjarni cited "how different the Movement's vision for the future is, compared to what I want to stand for".<ref name="collapse new">{{cite web |url=https://www.lemonde.fr/en/international/article/2024/10/13/iceland-s-government-coalition-collapses-new-elections-to-be-held-in-november_6729282_4.html |title=Iceland's government coalition collapses, new elections to be held in November |work=[[Le Monde]] |date=13 October 2024 |access-date=14 October 2024 |archive-date=10 November 2024 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20241110003617/https://www.lemonde.fr/en/international/article/2024/10/13/iceland-s-government-coalition-collapses-new-elections-to-be-held-in-november_6729282_4.html |url-status=live }}</ref>

==Issues==
In November 2024, a [[Gallup, Inc.|Gallup]] poll found that more than 60% of voters regarded healthcare, economic issues and housing as a top concern. Immigration was regarded as a key issue by 32% of those surveyed while asylum issues accounted for 18% of respondents. The public broadcaster [[RÚV]] said that an [[AI chatbot]] set up to answer election-related questions received mostly queries on housing and taxation.<ref>{{Cite web |date=30 November 2024 |title=Icelanders head to the polls after government collapse |url=https://www.france24.com/en/live-news/20241130-icelanders-head-to-the-polls-after-government-collapse |access-date=30 November 2024 |website=France 24 |language=en}}</ref>

== Electoral system ==
{{further|Elections in Iceland}}
The 63 members of the [[Althing]] are elected by [[open list]] [[proportional representation]] in six multi-member [[Constituencies of Iceland|constituencies]], with 54 seats allocated to parties at the constituency level with no [[electoral threshold]], and 9 [[leveling seat]]s assigned to party lists at the national level in order to ensure proportionality with the election result, with a threshold of 5 percent required.<ref name="OSCE">{{cite report|title=Ísland, Alþingiskosningar, 27. April 2013: Úttektarskýrsla|url=http://www.osce.org/is/odihr/elections/iceland/103314?download=true|publisher=OSCE/ODIHR|date=24 June 2013|access-date=30 October 2017|archive-date=14 September 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160914235149/http://www.osce.org/is/odihr/elections/iceland/103314?download=true|url-status=live}}</ref> The 54 constituency seats are distributed within each constituency according to the [[D'Hondt method]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Útreikningar við úthlutun jöfnunarsæta|url=http://www.kosning.is/upplysingar/jofnunarsaeti/|publisher=Kosningavefur Dómsmála- og Mannréttindaráðuneytisins|date=27 April 2013|access-date=30 October 2017|archive-date=2 November 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171102015341/http://www.kosning.is/upplysingar/jofnunarsaeti/|url-status=live}}</ref> Election lists are determined by parties; although voters have the option of altering the order of candidates or striking out particular candidates entirely, this rarely has any effect on the result.<ref name="OSCE"/>

The number of voters per parliamentary seat varies significantly between the constituencies of Iceland.{{cn|date=December 2024}}  The number of seats per constituency was determined by law in 1999 and is not updated before each election except when the number of voters per representative in the most represented constituency reaches half the number of voters per representative in the least represented constituency.{{cn|date=December 2024}}  The [[Constitution of Iceland|constitution]] stipulates that in this case, a constituency seat should be transferred from the most represented constituency to the least represented constituency.{{cn|date=December 2024}}  This has happened twice since 1999, in both cases transferring a constituency seat from the [[Northwest (Althing constituency)|Northwest]] constituency (a large rural constituency) to the [[Southwest (Althing constituency)|Southwest]] constituency (a relatively small constituency in area, adjoining the capital city).{{cn|date=December 2024}} In the 2021 election, the number of eligible voters for each seat in the Southwest was 5671.5, more than double the 2693.5 eligible voters for each seat in the Northwest. According to the constitution, this means another transfer of a constituency seat from the Northwest to the Southwest before the next parliamentary election.{{cn|date=December 2024}}

===Date===
Per Article 23 in Chapter V of Act No. 112 from the 25 June 2021 Elections Law, elections must be held no later than the same weekday of the month four years after the previous elections, counting from the turn of the month;<ref>{{cite web |title=112/2021: Kosningalög |url=https://www.althingi.is/lagas/nuna/2021112.html |access-date=28 May 2022 |publisher=Alþingi |archive-date=28 May 2022 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20220528200058/https://www.althingi.is/lagas/nuna/2021112.html |url-status=live }}</ref> therefore, because the [[2021 Icelandic parliamentary election|2021 election]] took place on the fourth Saturday in September, the latest possible date for the election was 27 September 2025.{{cn|date=December 2024}} 

Following the collapse of the government in October 2024, the election was held on 30 November.<ref name="collapse new" />

== Results ==
Results showed no party winning a majority in the Althing, with the [[Social Democratic Alliance]] outperforming the ruling [[Independence Party (Iceland)|Independence Party]] to win a plurality of seats.<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://apnews.com/article/iceland-election-results-social-democrats-989773ec8a24cddef7ba78d5aa933df2 |title=Social democrats gain and incumbents are punished in Iceland’s election |date=1 December 2024 |access-date=1 December 2024 |website=Associated Press |language=en |archive-date=1 December 2024 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20241201150818/https://apnews.com/article/iceland-election-results-social-democrats-989773ec8a24cddef7ba78d5aa933df2 |url-status=live }}</ref> The election saw the worst performance by the [[Independence Party (Iceland)|Independence Party]], [[Progressive Party (Iceland)|Progressive Party]], the [[Left-Green Movement]], and the [[Pirate Party (Iceland)|Pirate Party]] in each of the parties' histories, while [[Viðreisn]], the [[People's Party (Iceland)|People's Party]], and the [[Centre Party (Iceland)|Centre Party]] saw their best performance in each of the parties' histories. This follows a trend of Icelanders voting against every post [[Great Recession|2008 recession]] government except for [[2021 Icelandic parliamentary election|2021.]]<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.france24.com/en/live-news/20241201-social-democrats-set-to-overtake-ruling-party-in-iceland-snap-election |title=Social Democrats overtake PM's party in Iceland vote |date=1 December 2024 |access-date=1 December 2024 |website=France 24 |language=en |archive-date=1 December 2024 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20241201121640/https://www.france24.com/en/live-news/20241201-social-democrats-set-to-overtake-ruling-party-in-iceland-snap-election |url-status=live }}</ref>

The Left-Green Movement and the Pirate Party lost all of their parliamentary representation, with each failing to obtain a seat for the first time since their foundation in [[1999 Icelandic parliamentary election|1999]] and [[2013 Icelandic parliamentary election|2013]] respectively.<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2024/12/1/social-democrats-win-icelands-snap-election-as-incumbents-punished |title=Social Democrats win Iceland’s snap elections as incumbents punished |date=1 December 2024 |access-date=2 December 2024 |website=Al Jazeera |language=en}}</ref>

== Government formation ==
{{Main|Cabinet of Kristrún Frostadóttir}}
[[People's Party (Iceland)|People's Party]] leader [[Inga Sæland]] expressed an interest in joining a governing coalition and said she favored a coalition consisting of her own party, the [[Social Democratic Alliance]], and [[Viðreisn]]. However, she did not explicitly rule out any party. This followed with praise from the leader of the Social Democratic Alliance [[Kristrún Frostadóttir]] for Inga's work on repairing issues affecting the elderly and disabled.<ref name=coalition>{{Cite news|date=2024-12-01 |access-date=2024-12-02|title=Inga Sæland hallar sér upp að Kristrúnu|url=https://www.mbl.is/frettir/innlent/2024/12/01/inga_saeland_hallar_ser_upp_ad_kristrunu/|website=mbl.is}}</ref>

Kristrún Frostadóttir also expressed interest in forming a government, but did not rule out any party, but signaled that if she formed a coalition with the [[Independence Party (Iceland)|Independence Party]], she said they would need to change their policies. However, she said she preferred a coalition with those who were ideologically closer to her party. There was talk also about the possibility of forming the biggest parliamentary majority between the three largest parties (S, D, and C), similarly to what was done in the last parliamentary session.<ref name=coalition/>

Viðreisn's leader [[Þorgerður Katrín Gunnarsdóttir]] also expressed an interest in forming a government and said she was interested in joining a "coherent" government and was not seeking to become Prime Minister. Election results showed that her party could play a kingmaker role in who forms the next government and would be able to provide a majority (if it comes down) to either a possible centrist left-leaning coalition with the Social Democrats and People's or possibly with Independence and the [[Centre Party (Iceland)|Centre Party]], which is more right-leaning.<ref name=coalition/>

Centre Party leader [[Sigmundur Davíð Gunnlaugsson]] met with President [[Halla Tómasdóttir]] on 2 December. He stated that he believed that Social Democratic Alliance leader Kristrún Frostadóttir should get the first mandate to form a government, as her party won first and said he did not rule out joining a government with any party. He stated he would implement "real changes", if he does enter a coalition.<ref name=MCentre>{{Cite news|date=2 Dec 2024 |access-date=2024-12-02|title=Eðlilegt að Kristrún fái umboðið|url= https://www.mbl.is/frettir/innlent/2024/12/02/edlilegt_ad_kristrun_fai_umbodid/|website=mbl.is}}</ref> Independence Party leader and Prime Minister [[Bjarni Benediktsson (born 1970)|Bjarni Benediktsson]] also met with Halla and stated that he would not be making any special effort in asking for a mandate to forming a government, stating nothing was achieved in getting it and not being able to do anything with it, if received. He also stated he intended to meet with his party to decide on how to proceed, following the elections.<ref name=DIndependence>{{Cite news|date=2 Dec 2024 |access-date=2024-12-02|title= Discussed the possibilities in the situation|url= https://icelandmonitor.mbl.is/news/news/2024/12/02/discussed_the_possibilities_in_the_situation/ |website=mbl.is}}</ref> 

On 3 December, Halla gave Kristrún the first mandate and expressed interest in trying to form a government with her own party, Viðreisn, and the People's Party.<ref name=SDGov>{{Cite news|date=3 Dec 2024 |access-date=2024-12-03|title= Kristrún Frostadóttir Receives The Mandate To Form Government|url= https://grapevine.is/news/2024/12/03/kristrun-frostadottir-receives-the-mandate-to-form-government/ |website=grapevine.is}}</ref> On 4 December, Kristrún Frostadóttir began the formal process of forming a government consisting of Social Democratic Alliance, Viðreisn and the People's Party. If successfully formed, the government would have 36 of the 63 seats in the [[Althing]]. The coalition leaders were dubbed as "[[valkyrie]]s", citing that the three parties are led by women.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2024-12-04 |title=From Iceland — Kristrún Frostadóttir Begins Formal Government Formation |url=https://grapevine.is/news/2024/12/04/kristrun-frostadottir-begins-formal-government-formation/ |access-date=2024-12-04 |website=The Reykjavik Grapevine |language=en-US}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |date=2024-12-03 |title=Valkyrie Coalition: Iceland's New Political Era |url=https://www.devdiscourse.com/article/politics/3179905-valkyrie-coalition-icelands-new-political-era?amp |access-date=2024-12-17 |website=www.devdiscourse.com |language=en-US}}</ref>

On 21 December, Kristrún Frostadóttir formed [[Cabinet of Kristrún Frostadóttir|her cabinet]] and was appointed prime minister. The cabinet consists of four ministers from Kristrún's Social Democratic Alliance, four from Þorgerður's Viðreisn, and three from Inga's People's Party.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://icelandmonitor.mbl.is/news/news/2024/12/21/these_are_the_ministers_of_the_government/ |title=These are the ministers of the government |language=en |website=Iceland Monitor.is |author=Iceland Monitor|access-date=21 December 2024}}</ref>
