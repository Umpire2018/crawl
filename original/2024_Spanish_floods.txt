On 29 October 2024, [[torrential rain]] caused by an [[cut-off low|isolated low-pressure area at high levels]] brought over a year's worth of [[precipitation]] to several areas in eastern [[Spain]], including the [[Valencian Community]], [[Castilla–La Mancha]], and [[Andalusia]]. The resulting [[floodwater]]s caused the deaths of about 232 people, with three more missing<ref name="Spanish Government data update">{{cite news |date=4 January 2025 |title=Actualización de datos del Gobierno de España |trans-title=Spanish Government data update |url=https://www.lamoncloa.gob.es/info-dana/Paginas/2025/040125-datos-seguimiento-actuaciones-gobierno.aspx |access-date=4 January 2025 |newspaper=La Moncloa |language=es}}</ref> and substantial property damage.<ref>{{Cite news |date=2 November 2024 |first1=Holly |last1=Evans |first2=Tara |last2=Cobham |first3=Alex|last3=Croft |title=Spain floods latest: 5,000 more soldiers deployed as satellite photos show extent of devastation |url=https://www.independent.co.uk/news/world/europe/spain-floods-latest-valencia-map-rain-huelva-deaths-b2640194.html |access-date=2 November 2024 |work=[[The Independent]]}}</ref><ref name="TG211">{{cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2024/nov/02/spain-floods-disaster-5000-more-troops-drafted-in-to-deal-with-aftermath|title=Spain floods: 10,000 troops and police drafted in to deal with disaster|first=Sam|last=Jones|date=2 November 2024|work=[[The Guardian]]|access-date=2 November 2024}}</ref> It is one of the deadliest natural disasters in Spanish history.<ref>{{Cite news|title=Spain's deadliest floods in decades: Death toll reaches 205 as temporary morgue opens |work=[[ITVX]] |date=1 November 2024 |url=https://www.itv.com/news/2024-11-01/spain-floods-rescuers-search-for-missing-as-death-toll-hits-158 |access-date=1 November 2024 |first=James |last=Mates}}</ref>

Though similar torrential rain events had happened in the past in the region, the flooding was more intense, likely due to the [[effects of climate change]]. The poor preparation and [[disaster response]] of the [[Autonomous communities of Spain|regional]] and national governments also likely aggravated the human cost of the event, notably in [[Valencian Community|Valencia]]. After the flooding, thousands of volunteers from all around Spain and numerous nonprofit organizations mobilized to help with the cleanup and recovery.

== Background ==
Disastrous floods have been reported throughout the [[history of Valencia]], from the 14th century up to the contemporary period.<ref>{{Cite web |date=30 October 2024 |title=La primera gran riada en la historia de Valencia fue en 1517: derribó tres puentes de cinco y hubo cientos de muertos |trans-title=The first major flood in the history of Valencia was in 1517: it destroyed three of five bridges and left hundreds dead. |url=https://www.elespanol.com/historia/20241030/primera-gran-riada-historia-valencia-derribo-puentes-cientos-muertos/897410435_0.html |access-date=31 October 2024 |website=El Español |language=es |first=David |last=Barreira Randulfe}}</ref> The [[1957 Valencia flood]] was caused by a three-day [[cold drop]] ({{Langx|es|gota fría}}) (which usually leads to heavy autumn rains in Spain and France); it overflowed the banks of the [[Turia (river)|Túria river]] and devastated the city of [[Valencia]].<ref name="ABC">{{cite news |last1=Carrión |first1=R. |last2=Nicasio |first2=B. |date=13 August 2007 |title=Hasta aquí llegó la riada |trans-title=The flood reached this point |url=http://www.abc.es/hemeroteca/historico-13-08-2007/abc/Valencia/hasta-aqui-llego-la-riada_164364057827.html |access-date=31 October 2024 |website=[[ABC (newspaper)|ABC]] |language=es}}</ref><ref name="Gallardo">{{cite news |last1=Gallardo |first1=Cristina |last2=Niiler |first2=Eric |last3=De Avila |first3=Joseph |title=How Deadly Flooding Ravaged Spain's Valencia Region |url=https://www.wsj.com/world/europe/how-valencia-spain-flood-happened-347c9e25 |work=The Wall Street Journal |date=2 November 2024 |url-access=subscription}}</ref>

The 1957 flood resulted in at least 81 fatalities.<ref name="ABC" />  In response, the [[Fifth government of Francisco Franco|government]] of [[Francisco Franco]] initiated work on a "Southern Solution" ({{langx|es|Solución Sur}}), a giant canal to reroute the Túria River around the south of the city centre, three kilometres from its original course.<ref name="ABC" /> From 1964 to 1973, the Franco government built the new 12 kilometre long, 175 metre wide channel, capable of draining 5,000 cubic metres of water per second.<ref name="ABC" />  The Southern Solution was later revised from a hydraulic engineering project into a comprehensive Southern Plan ({{Langx|es|Plan Sur}}) which also included roads, railways, and other urban development projects.<ref name="ABC" />  Since then, the Southern Plan has shielded the city centre, but did nothing to protect the rapidly growing towns to the south.<ref name="Gallardo" /> In 2008, a hydraulic infrastructure program was proposed to protect the southern towns, at a cost of about €200 million.<ref name="Gallardo" /> Very little of that program was ever implemented, leaving those towns vulnerable to severe flooding.<ref name="Gallardo" />

In September 2019, floods killed six people in [[Vega Baja del Segura]]. To respond to future floods, the [[Second government of Ximo Puig|Valencian government]] of [[Ximo Puig]] established the Valencian Emergencies Unit ({{langx|ca-valencia|Unitat Valenciana d'Emergències}}; {{langx|es|Unidad Valenciana de Emergencias}}). After the [[2023 Valencian regional election]]. The new [[government of Carlos Mazón|Valencian government]] of [[Carlos Mazón]] shut down the unit, which it considered a "superfluous expense".<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.eldiario.es/comunitat-valenciana/politica/unidad-valenciana-emergencias-servicio-coordinacion-mazon-suspendio-llegar-gobierno_1_11779165.html|first=Laura|last=Martínez|date=30 October 2024|title=Qué era la Unidad Valenciana de Emergencias, el servicio de coordinación que Mazón suspendió al llegar al Gobierno|trans-title=What was the Valencian Emergency Unit, the coordination service that Mazón suspended when he took office?|work=[[elDiario.es]]|access-date=30 October 2024|language=es}}</ref>

On 25 October 2024, Spanish national agency [[AEMET]] meteorologist Juan Jesús González Alemán warned that the upcoming cold drop or [[Cut-off_low#DANA|DANA]] could become a high impact storm. This was initially ridiculed and described as "[[Media coverage of climate change#Alarmism|alarmism]]" on [[Twitter]] by [[climate change denial]]ists.<ref name="negationists-1">{{cite news|url=https://www.eldiario.es/rastreador/experto-aemet-aviso-cinco-dias-peligro-suponia-dana-ridiculizado-redes-sociales_132_11778978.html|title=Un experto de AEMET avisó hace cinco días del peligro que suponía esta DANA y fue ridiculizado en redes sociales|trans-title=An AEMET expert warned five days ago of the danger posed by this DANA and was ridiculed on social networks|date=30 October 2024|work=[[elDiario.es]]|language=es|access-date=31 October 2024}}</ref><ref name="negationists-2">{{cite news|url=https://cadenaser.com/nacional/2024/10/30/farsante-los-insultos-que-recibio-un-experto-de-la-aemet-al-avisar-del-riesgo-de-esta-dana-cadena-ser/|title=Un experto de la AEMET avisó hace cinco días del riesgo de la DANA y fue ridiculizado en redes|trans-title=An AEMET expert warned of the danger posed by this DANA five days ago and was ridiculed on social media|date=30 October 2024|work=[[Cadena SER]]|language=es|access-date=2 November 2024}}</ref>

==Environmental factors==
Four environmental factors combined to produce the devastation of the 2024 Spanish floods:

The first factor is that the [[urban area]] of the City of Valencia lies flat around a riverbed on an [[alluvial plain]], which places it at high risk of flooding. When heavy rains coincide with a [[convective storm]] during which the sea level tends to rise, the drainage of water is hindered and flood risk is doubled. The areas where it rained the most have not been flooded, but those whose geography is more predisposed to accumulate water, like the peripheral municipalities, but not the well protected city centre, were quickly flooded.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Sangrà |first=Marta |date=2024-10-31 |title=History and geography of Valencia: why have some areas been flooded and others not? |url=https://valenciasecreta.com/en/history-geography-valencia-dana/ |access-date=2024-11-04 |work=Valencia Secreta}}</ref>

The second factor is the [[urbanisation]] of the coastal area. [[Impervious surface]]s, such as roads and buildings,<ref name="Eguibar2021">{{Cite journal |last1=Eguíbar Galán |first1=Miguel Ángel |last2=Porta-García |first2=Raimon |last3=Torrijo Echarri |first3=Francisco Javier |last4=Garzón Roca |first4=Julio |date=2021-10-21 |title=Flood Hazards in Flat Coastal Areas of the Eastern Iberian Peninsula: A Case Study in Oliva (Valencia, Spain) |journal=Water |volume=13 |issue=21 |page=2975 |doi=10.3390/w13212975 |doi-access=free |bibcode=2021Water..13.2975E |url=https://docta.ucm.es/entities/publication/fe345b0f-3a35-4504-a05e-97e6e6e0b020 |issn=2073-4441}}</ref>{{rp|p=5}} impede water from penetrating the ground.{{r|Eguibar2021|p=12}} Water pools on level land,{{r|Eguibar2021|p=17}} and is further retained by the metre-high [[beach ridge]].{{r|Eguibar2021|p=117}} When runoff is prevented and water gathers, [[flash flood]]ing results.{{r|Eguibar2021|p=15}} Rising sea levels will further slow drainage time to days or even weeks.{{r|Eguibar2021|p=21}} From 1997–2007 there was intense urbanisation of the area around the City of Valencia and coastal areas generally, with little regard for the danger of severe flooding.

The third factor are the mountains of Valencia's interior.{{r|Eguibar2021|p=2}} Blocked from entering the interior of Spain by the mountains, humid air from over the sea is forced up by the wind, where it cools, condensing into heavy rain, producing torrents that can quickly flood low lying areas.{{r|Eguibar2021|pp=4-5}} For example, the town of [[Oliva]] south of the City of Valencia has experienced 20 intense floods since 1972.{{r|Eguibar2021|p=3}}

The fourth factor is that [[climate change]] had increased the destructive power of the storm [[Effects of climate change|in several ways]]. After the 2024 summer, the record high temperatures of the [[Mediterranean Sea]] and [[Atlantic Ocean]] caused unusually high levels of water evaporation, that precipitated with record intensity [[Cold drop|when it came into contact with high altitude cold air]] arriving from the north. It made the storm 2 times more likely and the rainfall 12% heavier.<ref>{{cite web |title=Devastating rainfall hits Spain in yet another flood-related disaster |url=https://wmo.int/media/news/devastating-rainfall-hits-spain-yet-another-flood-related-disaster |website=World Meteorologic Organization |date=31 October 2024 |access-date=17 November 2024}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|last=Hedgecoe|first=Guy|date=1 November 2024|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c62lkne32v9o|title=Trapped in cars and garages: Why Valencia floods proved so deadly|work=[[BBC News]]|access-date=4 November 2024}}</ref> Increasing the flood risk, the hotter recent summers made the soil drier,  reducing the ground's ability to soak up water, which facilitates flash flooding. Scientists suggest that climate change may also have caused the storm to move more slowly, creating more rainfall in the same place; the same is said about the central European [[2024 Central European floods|storm "Boris"]].<ref>{{cite news |last1=McGrath |first1=Matt |title=Scientists say climate change made Spanish floods worse |url=https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c98eylqeg06o |access-date=17 November 2024 |agency=BBC |date=30 October 2024}}</ref> The warmer sea waters also increase wind speed during such storms.<ref>{{cite web |title=expert reaction to flash floods in south-eastern Spain |url=https://www.sciencemediacentre.org/expert-reaction-to-flash-floods-in-south-eastern-spain/ |website=Science Media Centre |access-date=17 November 2024}}</ref> The [[Climate change in the Arctic|warming of the Arctic]] makes the [[jet stream]] weaker, which facilitated the movement of cold arctic air further south, as occurred in this instance, when a pocket of cold air from over Greenland penetrated the warm air mass over Spain. When the cold air from the north meets the warm humid air from over the sea, it creates intense rainfall and strong winds. Climate change warming has also extended the season in which this [[Cold drop|DANA phenomena]] can occur: from only November, to anytime in the year.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Ruiz de Elvira Serra |first1=Antonio |title=Valencia floods: Our warming climate is making once-rare weather more common, and more destructive |url=https://theconversation.com/valencia-floods-our-warming-climate-is-making-once-rare-weather-more-common-and-more-destructive-242798 |access-date=17 November 2024 |agency=The conversation |date=5 November 2024}}</ref>

[[Climate Central]] [[Extreme event attribution|extreme event attribution analysis]] indicates that the unprecedented intensity of the rainfalls were largely due to the [[Effects of climate change on oceans#Rising ocean temperature|rising temperature]] of the [[Atlantic Ocean]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.climatecentral.org/climate-shift-index-alert/ocean-csi-spain-floods|title=Analysis: Valencia floods fueled by ocean warming in the Tropical Atlantic linked to climate change|date=31 October 2024|work=[[Climate Central]]|access-date=31 October 2024}}</ref>

As of November, the Valencian government had not declared the situation a "catastrophic emergency", the maximum [[State of emergency#Spain|state of emergency]] available to a regional government, which would have made Mazón personally and legally responsible for managing the emergency response, thus keeping both political control at the regional level and legal blame to [[Generalitat Valenciana|regional Minister of the Interior]] {{ill|Salomé Pradas Ten|es||ca}},<ref>{{cite web|first1=Miguel|last1=González|first2=Ferran|last2=Bono|url=https://elpais.com/espana/2024-11-02/la-generalitat-valenciana-no-ha-declarado-el-maximo-nivel-de-emergencia-que-permite-su-legislacion.html|title=La Generalitat valenciana no ha declarado el máximo nivel de emergencia que permite su legislación|trans-title=The Valencian Government has not declared the maximum level of emergency permitted by legislation|date=2 November 2024|work=[[El País]]|language=es|access-date=2 November 2024}}</ref> who was dismissed by Mazón on 22 November.{{citation needed|date=November 2024}}

On 4 November, in an interview with [[Cadena COPE]], Mazón blamed the Spanish government and commanders of the [[Military Emergencies Unit]] (UME) for mismanaging the response to the flooding. He claimed that, at 15:21 on 29 October, he had requested the intervention of the UME in Utiel and Requena, and that the [[List of agencies in Spain#Directorate-General for Water|Hydrographic Confederation]], under the direction of Environment Minister [[Teresa Ribera]], had suppressed the alerts. This was swiftly denied by the Confederation, which stated that the regional government is responsible for issuing any alerts.<ref>{{cite web|first=Laura|last=Martínez|date=4 November 2024|url=https://www.eldiario.es/comunitat-valenciana/politica/mazon-cambia-estrategia-busca-confrontacion-sanchez-culpa-ahora-ume-confederacion-hidrografica-retraso_1_11789229.html|title=Mazón cambia de estrategia y busca la confrontación con Sánchez: culpa ahora a la UME y a la Confederación Hidrográfica del retraso|trans-title=Mazón changes strategy and seeks confrontation with Sánchez: he now blames the UME and the Hydrographic Confederation for the delay|work=[[elDiario.es]]|language=es|access-date=4 November 2024}}</ref> General Francisco Javier Marcos, the commander of the UME, also rejected Mazón's claims. He stated that the UME had mobilised 1,000 soldiers on the day of the floods, but that it was subject to the orders of the regional government, which directed the emergency response. He said the moment the Valencian government gave the order, the UME deployed to affected zones within minutes.<ref>{{cite web|first=José Enrique|last=Monrosi|date=4 November 2024|url=https://www.eldiario.es/politica/jefe-ume-desmiente-mazon-comunidad-autonoma-dirige-emergencia-decide_1_11790706.html|title= El jefe de la UME desmiente a Mazón: "Es la comunidad autónoma quien dirige la emergencia y decide dónde vamos"|trans-title=The head of the UME refutes Mazón: "It is the autonomous community who directs the emergency and decides where we go"|language=es|work=[[elDiario.es]]|access-date=4 November 2024}}</ref>

On 20 November, Mazón appointed the retired military officer and former director of the [[Spanish Armed Forces Intelligence Center]] {{ill|Francisco José Gan Pampols|es|}} as the new Vice President of the Valencian Government for Reconstruction.<ref>{{cite news |title=Mazón pone a un militar al frente de la reconstrucción económica y social |first=Manuel |last=Viejo González |url=https://elpais.com/espana/2024-11-19/mazon-sorprende-con-el-fichaje-de-un-militar-en-la-reserva-para-la-vicepresidencia-de-la-reconstruccion.html |date=19 November 2024 |trans-title=Mazón names a military office head of the social and economic reconstruction |work=El País |language=es}}</ref>

Four people were detained, and thirty-one police officers were wounded.<ref>{{Cite news |date=2024-11-10 |title=Una marcha multitudinaria pide en Valencia la dimisión de Mazón por dejar al pueblo "abandonado a su suerte" |trans-title=A multitudinary march asks in Valencia for Mazón's resignation for leaving the people "abandoned to their own devices" |url=https://www.rtve.es/noticias/20241109/valencia-manifestacion-pide-dimision-mazon-gestion-dana/16323642.shtml |access-date=2024-11-10 |work=RTVE |language=es}}</ref> Angry protesters were seen clashing with riot police in front of [[Valencia City Hall]], while others waved signs and banners with messages such as "You killed us" and "Our hands are stained with mud, yours with blood".<ref name="Boyle">{{cite news |last1=Boyle |first1=Cash |title=Thousands call for Valencia's leader to resign over deadly floods response |url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2024/nov/09/thousands-call-for-valencias-leader-to-resign-over-deadly-floods-response |access-date=16 November 2024 |work=The Guardian |date=9 November 2024}}</ref>

===Environmentalists===
In [[Edinburgh]], [[Scotland]], an environmentalist group defaced [[SUV]]s in the city with the message "These cars kill Valencians" and left photographs of flood victims on the cars' windscreens. The group held SUV drivers partially responsible for the deaths caused in the floods, stating that "If SUVs were a country they’d be the 5th biggest world polluter".<ref>{{cite web|last=Gayle|first=Damien|date=4 November 2024|url=https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2024/nov/04/edinburgh-activists-tyre-extinguishers-target-suvs-in-solidarity-with-spains-flood-victims|title=Edinburgh activists target SUVs in solidarity with Spain's flood victims|work=[[The Guardian]]|access-date=5 November 2024}}</ref>

===European Parliament===
During her hearings for her [[von der Leyen Commission II|commissioner]]’s role, the [[European People's Party]] questioned [[Teresa Ribera]] over the management of the disastrous flash floods in Valencia, accusing her of ignoring the needs to update, drain and improve the [[Rambla del Poyo]] as the head of the [[Ministry of Environment (Spain)|Ministry of Environment]].<ref>{{Cite web |date=2024-11-12 |title=El ministerio de Ribera frenó la reforma del barranco del Poyo en 2020 |url=https://www.telemadrid.es/programas/telenoticias-1/El-ministerio-de-Ribera-freno-la-reforma-del-barranco-del-Poyo-en-2020-2-2724347557--20241112030641.amp.html |access-date=2024-11-15 |website=Telemadrid |language=es}}</ref> Despite this initial opposition, her appointment was approved by [[European Parliament]] on 27 November 2024.
